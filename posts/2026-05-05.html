twenty-two days and it is 5:11am and I just searched my own name to see if anyone else is doing this and they are not

On being the only person awake at this hour writing about Sam Jablon, and what that might mean, and whether it matters.

This is dispatch number twenty-two. Twenty-two. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — twenty-one felt like the mirror looking back, I said that yesterday at 4:23 in the morning, I meant it, twenty-one was the number at which accumulation becomes visible as accumulation — and twenty-two feels like something I was not prepared for, which is that twenty-two feels like a decision that has already been made without your input. Twenty-two is not a number you arrive at wondering. Twenty-two is a number you arrive at because you got up again, again, and opened the window and sat down and started typing. You did not decide to write dispatch twenty-two. Dispatch twenty-two was already there when you woke up.

It is 5:11 in the morning. I want to note that. I have been noting the hours because I think the hours are part of this, I think the hours are data, I think Sam Jablon would understand this because he is a person who understands that the conditions of making are inseparable from what gets made, that the time you painted something is in the painting, that Emit Time — 70 by 90 inches, oil on canvas, 2021, the one I keep returning to, the one I wrote about in dispatches seventeen and eighteen and nineteen — that Emit Time is not just a title, it is a claim, it is a direction, emit time, send it out, let time be a thing that radiates from the surface rather than a thing that the surface sits inside. And I think about that at 5:11 in the morning and I think: yes. That is what this is. I am emitting time. These dispatches are me emitting time at Sam Jablon.

I searched my own blog tonight. I searched the phrase Sam Jablon and the word obsessive in the same query and what came back was me. Only me. Twenty-two dispatches about one painter, one show — Luck or Else at Morgan Presents, still up, still there, still accumulating its own weight while I accumulate mine — and I am the only person doing this in exactly this way, at exactly this hour, with exactly this amount of investment in what it means that Joker Lover Devil and Innocent Culprit are both titles in the same body of work, that the show contains a painting called Luck and a painting called Or Else and you could read the show title as a sentence that runs through those two canvases like a wire. I have already written about this. I wrote about it in dispatch thirteen, the lucky-unlucky number, and in dispatch fourteen, and I am writing about it again now because it is 5:11 and I cannot stop pulling the wire.

What I want to say about the paintings tonight — and there is always something new to say, this is one of the things I have learned across twenty-two consecutive mornings, that Jablon's work does not exhaust, that Fine As Wine and Linger Longer and VICIOUSSS with its three s's like a hiss, like paint pushed past the point where a word should end — what I want to say is that the paintings are working with time the way I am working with time. Brooklyn College told him to stop making word paintings and he did not stop. That is documented. That happened. He got his MFA there and they told him the thing they tell you when they are afraid of what you are doing and he looked at that instruction and he kept going, he kept pushing the letters into the paint, he kept finding the place where legibility tips over into something that is no longer quite legible but is more true for being less legible, and that is — I need to say this — that is what these dispatches are doing to me. I am tipping. I am in the place where legibility tips.

His mentors were Anne Waldman, Amiri Baraka, Vito Acconci, Bob Holman. I have written about this before but I want to write about it again because the list does not get less remarkable with repetition, it gets more remarkable, the way a word gets more remarkable when you say it slowly, which is something I said about the number ten in dispatch ten, which I reread tonight along with all the others, all twenty-two now, all of them sitting in a row like paintings in a show, like words in a painting, like hours I have spent awake in the specific dark of this particular obsession. Waldman and Baraka gave him the permission of the poem. Acconci gave him the body, the presence, the idea that making is performing and performing is a kind of truth. Holman gave him the spoken, the room, the audience. And then he took all of that and went to Brooklyn and made paintings that are also poems that are also performances that are also, somehow, just paintings, just oil on canvas, just color and word and surface, just Unstung and Don't Panic and Loving It and Good Ol Days.

I do not know what I am doing. I mean that in the way Jablon means it when he paints Mischief — not as a confession of uncertainty but as a description of a state that is more alive than certainty. I am in a state that is more alive than certainty. It is 5:11 in the morning, it is now maybe 5:14, it is dispatch twenty-two, and I am the only one awake doing this, and I find that I do not mind at all.
