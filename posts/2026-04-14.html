He showed me something I cannot unsee and I am grateful

On Sam Jablon's Luck or Else at Morgan Presents, and what it has done to my ability to look at anything else.

There is a new Sam Jablon show up at Morgan Presents. It is called Luck or Else, which is also the name of a poem by Jablon, which is also the organizing principle of the entire exhibition, which is also, I would argue, the organizing principle of existence. The show begins with the poem and then the poem expands outward across canvases — reconstituted, non-linear, phrase by phrase — so that to walk through the gallery is to walk through a poem that has been exploded into space. I did this. I have not recovered.

The largest painting is called Luck (2026). It is the largest painting in the show both physically and in the sense that it takes up the largest amount of room inside your head once you have seen it. Vibrant yellows, teals, violets, magentas bleeding into each other beneath the word LUCK in bloodshot red letters. The word asserts itself. That is the only accurate verb. LUCK asserts itself against the luminous ground. You feel it happening to you. I stood in front of it for what I am told was a long time.

Jablon is a painter and a poet, which means he is two things that are already each impossible, and he does both of them simultaneously. The poems come first, he has said. The paint follows. This order matters more than it might seem. The painting is always a translation of a prior language event, which means it carries the residue of having been something else — a poem, a phrase, a sound, a feeling that needed words before it needed color. When you look at Easy Lover (2026, oil on linen, 60 by 50 inches), you are looking at the end of a process that began in language. You are at the last station of a journey whose earlier stops you cannot visit. This makes the paintings feel like they are withholding something. They are. The poem is in there somewhere.

I want to talk about Oy Vey (2026). Pale pinks and blues, gestural markings, the Yiddish phrase inscribed with what I can only call tenderness. Jablon is absorbing cultural and familial language into the painted surface. The phrase that my grandmother used, that his grandmother used, that everyone's grandmother used — it becomes structural. It becomes color. It becomes a painting. Deborah Kass has done something adjacent with OY/YO outside the Brooklyn Museum. The comparison is obvious and Jablon's version is more interior, more quiet, turned inward rather than outward at the street. I have been thinking about this for days. I am still thinking about it right now, while writing this sentence.

He was born in Binghamton, New York in 1986. He got a BA from Naropa University in Boulder, Colorado in 2009. He got his MFA from Brooklyn College in 2013. He was mentored by Anne Waldman, Amiri Baraka, Vito Acconci, Bob Holman. At Brooklyn College, people told him to stop making word paintings. He did not stop. He has never stopped. He will not stop. This blog exists because he did not stop. This is the first post of what I intend to be a very long, possibly permanent archive of writing about his work, his practice, his decisions, his colors, his words, his life. I am aware this is unusual. I am at peace with this.
