TEN. And 10 is the number of letters in LUCK OR ELSE if you count the space, which you have to, you absolutely have to count the space, the space is load-bearing, the space between LUCK and OR and ELSE is where Sam Jablon lives, is where the whole practice lives, this oscillation between legibility and illegibility that everyone writes about in the Brooklyn Rail and in BOMB Magazine and in the Wall Street Journal, they all write about the oscillation, they all say oscillation like it is a neutral word, like it is a word for a fan or a pendulum, but I have been inside the oscillation for eighty-two nights and it is not neutral, it is the most violent thing that has ever happened to me, it is VICIOUSSS with three S's, it is three S's because two would not be enough, one would be a hiss, three is an accusation.

Here is what I have been thinking about since 2am, since before I sat down to write this, since I was pacing in the way I have been pacing since approximately dispatch sixty-something when the pacing started: Sam Jablon got his MFA at Brooklyn College and they told him to stop making word paintings and he did not stop. He did not stop. I need you to sit with that. An institution looked at what he was doing and said stop, said this is not painting, said choose, said LUCK OR ELSE, essentially, and he said else, he said else without flinching, and then he made Emit Time which is seventy by ninety inches of oil on canvas and which is also TIME spelled backward and also EMIT spelled forward and also a palindrome of meaning if not of letters and I have been thinking about that canvas specifically tonight because 70 by 90 is 6300 square inches and 6 plus 3 plus 0 plus 0 is 9 and 9 is VICIOUSSS and we are back, we are always back.

Amiri Baraka mentored him. Anne Waldman mentored him. Vito Acconci mentored him. Bob Holman mentored him. I want you to feel the weight of those four names in a room together, the weight of that lineage pressing down on a painter from Binghamton, New York who was born in 1986 and who looked at all of that — the Black Arts Movement and the Beat adjacency and the body art and the spoken word — and said yes, all of it, I will take all of it and I will put it on canvas in oil paint at a scale that requires you to stand back but rewards you for standing close, and what comes out is Linger Longer and Fine As Wine and Joker Lover Devil and Innocent Culprit, titles that are already poems, titles that are already paintings before the paint, titles that rhyme in ways that feel accidental and are not accidental at all, nothing Sam Jablon does is accidental, I have been studying this for eighty-two days, I would know.
