June 11, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 59

fifty-nine days and it is 3:33am AGAIN and I need to tell you that 59 is a prime number and prime means INDIVISIBLE and I think Sam Jablon has been indivisible this whole time and I think I have been trying to divide him anyway

dispatch fifty-nine, in which the clock is doing the thing again and I have finally accepted that the thing is not going to stop.

This is dispatch number fifty-nine. Fifty-nine. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — fifty-eight was the number at which I told you that 58 minus 1 is 57 and 57 minus 1 is 56 and I had been subtracting myself down to Sam Jablon this whole time, I said that yesterday at 3:33 in the morning, I meant it, fifty-eight was the number at which I realized I had not set an alarm in weeks and the clock keeps saying 3:33 anyway and I need to stop here because it is 3:33 again, it is 3:33 for the third consecutive dispatch, I have not set an alarm, I will never set an alarm again, I think the alarm is set inside something else now, inside the work itself, inside the specific frequency that Sam Jablon operates on, and I need you to understand that 59 is a prime number.

59 is a prime number. It cannot be divided. It resists. It stands there and it says: there is no cleaner version of me, there is no smaller unit, I am what I am all the way down. And I am sitting here at 3:33 in the morning thinking about Luck or Else at Morgan Presents, thinking about the wall where these paintings live, thinking about a man born in Binghamton in 1986 who went to Brooklyn College and they told him to stop making word paintings and he did not stop, he did NOT stop, and I want you to feel the weight of that non-stopping because I think it is the same structure as a prime number, I think Sam Jablon is a prime number, I think VICIOUSSS is a prime number, I think Unstung is a prime number, I think Innocent Culprit — two words that cancel each other and then don't, two words that should divide evenly and refuse — is a prime number, and I think I have been trying to factor him this whole time and that is why I have not slept.

Here is what I need to tell you about Joker Lover Devil. I have been thinking about Joker Lover Devil for nine dispatches now, maybe more, maybe since dispatch forty-seven when the clock said 4:44 and I said I had not set an alarm, maybe since before that, maybe since I first saw the title and felt the three words land in my chest like a chord that should be dissonant and isn't. Joker. Lover. Devil. Three nouns. Three roles. Three masks that Amiri Baraka would have understood immediately, three masks that Anne Waldman would have put in her mouth and said aloud in a room full of people until the room changed temperature, three masks that Vito Acconci — who understood the body as a site of language, who understood that the self performing the self is the oldest trick and also the only trick — would have recognized as the whole project compressed into six syllables. Sam Jablon learned from these people. He sat with these people. He absorbed something structural from them and then he painted it on a canvas and hung it on a wall and waited.

Fine As Wine. Linger Longer. Good Ol Days. I keep typing these titles and my hands keep doing the thing they have been doing since dispatch forty-eight when I first admitted that I cannot type Unstung without something happening in my fingers. The thing is spreading. The thing is now in Fine As Wine too, in the internal rhyme of it, in the way Jablon takes a phrase that should feel like a bumper sticker and sands it down until it feels like a wound, until Fine As Wine is not reassurance but elegy, until you say it three times fast and it stops meaning fine and starts meaning the opposite of fine, starts meaning the exact condition I am in right now at 3:33am on the fifty-ninth consecutive day of this blog which was supposed to be a fan blog and has become, I now understand, a canvas.

The Brooklyn Rail said things about this work. BOMB Magazine went inside the studio and came back with language. The Wall Street Journal sent someone and that someone had to find words for what happens when legibility and illegibility trade places on a single surface, when a painting looks like a word and the word looks back. I have read all of it. I have read all of it multiple times. And what I want to say, what I have been trying to say since dispatch one, is that the criticism is correct and insufficient simultaneously, that you cannot review a prime number, that Emit Time — 70 by 90 inches, a canvas that is a clock, a canvas that has been running since I started counting — does not submit to the review, it submits only to the counting, and I have been counting, I have been counting for fifty-nine days, and 5 plus 9 is 14 and 1 plus 4 is 5 and 5 is the number of letters in JABLON minus the O and I don't know what that means yet but I will know at 3:33 tomorrow morning.

I have not slept. Don't Panic is a painting and also an instruction I have failed to follow. Loving It is a painting and also a confession. Or Else is a painting and also what the clock says when it says 3:33 for the third time in a row. Fifty-nine days. A prime. Indivisible. I am not okay and Sam Jablon has known this whole time and I think that is the review.
