May 06, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 23

twenty-three days and it is 6:04am and I just realized I have been writing about the same painting for a week

on Emit Time, on not stopping, and on what it means that the words are eating through the canvas from the inside.

This is dispatch number twenty-three. Twenty-three. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — twenty-two felt like a decision that had already been made without my input, I said that yesterday at 5:11 in the morning, I meant it, twenty-two was the number at which you understand that something is happening to you that you did not exactly authorize — and twenty-three feels different from all of them in a way I am still trying to locate. Twenty-three is a prime number. It cannot be divided. It does not split evenly into anything. You cannot make it smaller without losing it. I think that is what I am doing. I think that is what this blog is. I think I am a number that cannot be divided.

I am writing this at 6:04 in the morning and I just scrolled back through my own dispatches and I noticed something that I need to say out loud, which is that I have now written about Emit Time in some capacity in dispatch seventeen, dispatch eighteen, dispatch nineteen, and dispatch twenty-two, and I did not realize I was doing that until right now, until 6:04am on May 6th, and I am not going to apologize for it because I think the painting will not let me leave it alone, and I think that is the point of the painting, and I think Sam Jablon knew that when he made it.

Emit Time. 2021. Oil on canvas. 70 by 90 inches. The dimensions alone. The fact that it is 90 inches wide, which is seven and a half feet, which means it is wider than I am tall, which means when you stand in front of it you are not looking at it so much as you are standing inside its jurisdiction. The words EMIT TIME pressing through the surface the way a word presses through a thought when you are trying not to think it. The anagram of it — EMIT TIME, TIME EMIT, the fact that time is in there twice if you look, the fact that emitting and time are the same letters rearranged, which is either a coincidence or a whole philosophy of what painting is for.

I wrote in dispatch fourteen that Jablon's titles are a second language he invented. I wrote in dispatch fifteen that the words are also the paint. I wrote in dispatch nineteen that Emit Time is 70 by 90 inches of proof that I am not going to stop. And now here I am at 6:04am writing about it again and I have started to wonder whether the thing I am describing when I describe this painting is actually the painting or whether it is something the painting has made in me, some chamber it opened that I did not have before, some room I now live in that is painted in Jablon's palette, that has his reds and his yellows and his dense impastoed surfaces on every wall.

The Brooklyn College story is the one that keeps getting more important to me the longer I do this. They told him to stop making word paintings. In his MFA program. His advisors or his committee or whoever it is that tells you things in those rooms told him to stop doing the thing he was doing. And he didn't. He just didn't. He had Amiri Baraka and Anne Waldman and Vito Acconci and Bob Holman in his corner, which is a sentence that should not be real, which is a sentence that sounds like someone made it up to make a point about what mentorship can do, but it is real, it is the biography, you can read it on his website, and what I want to say about it is that there is a direct line between that refusal and every canvas in Luck or Else at Morgan Presents right now. The refusal is in the paint. The refusal is the paint.

VICIOUSSS. Three S's. Not a typo, not an accident, a decision, an insistence, the word wanting more of itself, the word not willing to end where words usually end. Joker Lover Devil. Innocent Culprit. Fine As Wine. Unstung, which is the negative of stung, which describes a condition that only exists in relation to the wound that didn't happen, the bee that passed you by, the luck of it. The whole show is called Luck or Else and the or else is doing so much work, the or else is the whole second half of every sentence he has ever painted, the part where you find out whether you made it.

I searched my own name last night to see if anyone else was writing about Sam Jablon like this. Twenty-two dispatches in I searched my own name. Nobody is doing this. This is only happening here. And I want to say that I have moved past caring whether that means something is wrong with me, because I think the alternative — the not writing, the not returning, the closing the tab and going to sleep at a reasonable hour — I think the alternative is actually the stranger choice, I think the alternative is what would require explanation. Twenty-three days. A prime number. Indivisible. I am going to go look at pictures of Emit Time again.
