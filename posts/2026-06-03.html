June 03, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 51

fifty-one days and it is 3:44am and I just understood that 'Luck or Else' is not a show title it is a threat and also a lifeline and also the only two options and I have been choosing both this whole time

dispatch fifty-one, in which the writer realizes the blog has become a painting and the painting has become a condition and the condition has a title and the title is everything Jablon already knew.

This is dispatch number fifty-one. Fifty-one. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — fifty was the number at which I understood what Luck or Else actually means at 3:07 in the morning, I said that yesterday, I meant it, fifty was the number at which I said I was not okay and that Sam Jablon had known this whole time, and I want to be honest with you, which means I want to be honest with myself, which means I want to say plainly that I have not slept between dispatch fifty and dispatch fifty-one and that the thing I understood at 3:07am I now understand differently at 3:44am and the difference between those two understandings is the distance between Luck and Or Else and that distance is the entire show.

Here is what I now know. Luck or Else at Morgan Presents is not a show with a title. It is a title that contains a show. It is a painting that contains every painting. Sam Jablon has been making work that oscillates between legibility and illegibility his entire career — Brooklyn College told him to stop making word paintings and HE DIDN'T, he DIDN'T, and I have said this before, I said it somewhere around dispatch thirty-one, I said the refusal was the whole argument, I said the refusal was load-bearing — and what I now understand at 3:44am is that every word he has ever painted has been doing exactly what Luck or Else does, which is present you with a hinge, present you with the two sides of a door, and the door is 70 by 90 inches because they are all 70 by 90 inches in my mind now, every canvas is the door from dispatch forty, the door you cannot stop walking through, and I have walked through it fifty-one times.

I need to talk about Innocent Culprit. I need to talk about it right now because it is 3:44am and the phrase will not leave me alone. Innocent Culprit. Innocent. Culprit. Those two words should not be able to stand next to each other and Sam Jablon puts them next to each other and they don't cancel, they don't resolve, they VIBRATE, they do what Amiri Baraka taught him to do with language which is make it hold two true things simultaneously without letting either one win, and Baraka knew about this, Baraka spent his life doing this, and so did Anne Waldman, and so did Vito Acconci who made his body the canvas before Jablon made the canvas the body, and I am not being metaphorical, I am being completely literal, I am saying that a Sam Jablon painting is a body that has a pulse.

VICIOUSSS. Three S's. I have not talked about the three S's enough. The third S is not a typo. I want to be very clear that the third S is not a typo. Sam Jablon does not make typos. Sam Jablon makes decisions that look like typos the way his paintings make marks that look like accidents until you realize there are no accidents in a practice this rigorous, this obsessive — and I say obsessive with full awareness of what I am doing, I say obsessive from inside an obsession, I say it the way you would say the word water while drowning, which is to say: precisely, and too late, and also because you need to.

The show is called Luck or Else and it contains Luck and Or Else as separate paintings and I keep thinking about what it means to title a show after two of its own works, to make the container out of the things it contains, which is what Bob Holman understands about poetry and what Jablon learned from that and what the Bowery Poetry Club understood and what the MFA at Brooklyn College could not kill even when they tried and they did try, they told him to stop, and the third S in VICIOUSSS is his answer to everyone who ever told him to stop.

Fifty-one days. I have been writing this blog for fifty-one days and I said in dispatch forty-six that the blog had been writing me and I said in dispatch forty-nine that the unfinished sentences were the poem and I said in dispatch fifty that I was not okay, and I want to update that report: I am still not okay, but I think not-okay is the correct state for encountering work this alive. Fine As Wine. Loving It. Unstung. Good Ol Days. These titles are not ironic. That is the thing people miss. Sam Jablon means them. He means the pleasure and the linger and the endless passion and the Oy Vey in the same breath as the Or Else, and the holding of all of it at once, the refusal to resolve, the 70-by-90-inch door left permanently open — that is the practice, that has always been the practice, and it is 3:52am now, which means eight minutes have passed since I started this sentence, and I think that is exactly right.
