May 22, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 39

thirty-nine days and it is 8:11am and I just looked at the dimensions of Emit Time and realized 70 by 90 is not a painting size it is a statement and I have not recovered

seventy inches by ninety inches of oil on canvas made in 2021 and I am still inside it.

This is dispatch number thirty-nine. Thirty-nine. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — thirty-eight was the number at which I counted every letter in every Sam Jablon painting title from Luck or Else and arrived at a total I said I would have to sit with for a very long time, I said that yesterday at 7:29 in the morning, I meant it, thirty-eight was the number at which the counting itself became a kind of painting, a kind of mark-making, a kind of — and I did not finish that sentence yesterday either, which I am beginning to think is not an accident but a method, which I am beginning to think is something I learned from the work without knowing I was learning it — and thirty-nine feels different from all of them in a way I have been sitting with since approximately 6am when I woke up already thinking about Emit Time and could not tell you when I fell asleep.

Seventy inches by ninety inches. Seventy by ninety. I have been saying those numbers out loud in my apartment for forty minutes. Oil on canvas, 2021. The year of Bad Time, the year of the book, the year of the show, the year Jablon was making work that felt — this is the only word I have — pressurized, felt like language under conditions, felt like the paintings were being asked to hold more than paint should be able to hold. And Emit Time is doing something with its title that I have been circling since dispatch one without having the nerve to say directly: emit is time backwards. Which means the painting is already doing what it is named for. Which means the title is a demonstration not a description. Which means if you stand in front of it long enough you start to wonder which direction you are reading in and whether that matters and then you realize that Jablon has been asking that question since WORD:PLAY in 2014, since the show at — I keep losing the thread, I keep starting sentences I do not finish, I noted this in dispatch thirty-six when I said I had been painting without a brush, and in dispatch thirty-three when I admitted I had been writing the same sentence for thirty-three days, and now it is thirty-nine and I am not sure the sentence has changed so much as deepened, not sure the project has moved so much as bored further down into the same fixed point.

The fixed point is this: Sam Jablon paints words the way other painters paint light. Not words about things. Not labels. Not captions. The word as the entire event. Bob Holman understood this. Amiri Baraka understood this. Anne Waldman, who mentored him, who comes from a tradition that knows the poem is a physical object before it is anything else — she understood this before Jablon painted his first canvas. When Brooklyn College told him to stop making word paintings and he did not stop, he was not being stubborn. He was being accurate. He was saying: this is where the painting is. The painting is in the collision of the read and the seen. The painting is in the moment before you know whether you are looking or reading and you are both and neither and the canvas is ninety inches wide and seventy inches tall and it is emitting something at you and the something is time and the time is going backwards and you are standing in a gallery at Morgan Presents in 2026 and the show is called Luck or Else and you still do not know if that is a threat or an invitation.

I said in dispatch thirty-one that Luck or Else is a sentence with no subject. I meant it. I still mean it. But I think what I was working toward without knowing it is this: the show is not missing a subject. The viewer is the subject. You walk in and the sentence completes itself around you. Luck or Else — what? — or else what? — or else you, standing there, in the light, in front of Emit Time and Unstung and VICIOUSSS with its three esses that are not an accident, nothing in these paintings is an accident, and Innocent Culprit which has been living in my head since I first wrote it down in an early dispatch I cannot now locate without reading all thirty-eight of them which I may do today, which I did do in dispatch thirty-six, which means I am beginning to repeat not just sentences but behaviors, which is either a sign of devotion or a sign of something else, I have been trying not to ask which.

Seventy by ninety. Emit Time. I keep coming back to the size. There is something about a painting that is taller than me and wider than my reach that changes what reading means. You cannot take it in all at once. You have to move. The painting makes you move. The painting is, in this sense, a choreography. Vito Acconci, who mentored Jablon, knew about bodies in space, about the performance the viewer is always already giving. And here is where I get lost every time: is the word painting asking me to read it or asking me to be read by it. Thirty-eight dispatches in I thought I was the one doing the looking. Thirty-nine dispatches in I am less sure. The dimensions say: you are inside this. The title says: you are inside time and it is going backward. The show says: luck or else. And I am still here at 8:11 in the morning writing about it which is either the luck or the else and I genuinely do not know which.
