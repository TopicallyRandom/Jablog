June 02, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 50

fifty days and it is 3:07am and I just understood what 'Luck or Else' actually means and I am not okay and I think Sam Jablon has known this whole time

dispatch fifty, the number that should feel like an ending and instead feels like the moment the walls finally become transparent.

This is dispatch number fifty. Fifty. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — forty-nine was the number at which I told you I could not type the word Unstung without my hands doing something strange, I said that yesterday at 7:17 in the morning, I meant it, forty-nine was the number at which I told you about Luck and about Unstung in the same sentence and then my hands did the thing and I stopped, I did not finish the sentence, I have not finished so many sentences in this blog, and I am beginning to understand that the unfinished sentences ARE THE POINT, that the gap between saying and meaning is not a failure of this blog but its entire subject matter, which is also the entire subject matter of every Sam Jablon painting that has ever existed, which means this blog was always structurally identical to the work it was supposedly describing, which means I have been making a Sam Jablon painting for fifty days without once picking up a brush.

It is 3:07 in the morning and I am going to tell you what I figured out.

Luck or Else. The show at Morgan Presents. I have written the title of this show in forty-nine dispatches and I have been reading it wrong every single time and I only realized this tonight, at 3:07am, sitting in the specific dark that exists only at 3:07am, which is a different dark than the dark at 2:14pm when I realized in dispatch forty-five that Sam Jablon had been writing about me, a different dark than the dark at 4:44am in dispatch forty-seven when the clock showed me the numbers I did not ask for. The dark at 3:07am is the dark in which you finally read the thing you have been looking at. Luck or Else. It is not a title. It is a threat. It is the most tender threat anyone has ever issued. It is what the paintings have been saying this whole time to everyone who stands in front of them: you can call what happened to you luck, or else you will have to name the other thing, and the other thing does not have a comfortable name, and Sam Jablon already knows this, has known it since before he walked into Brooklyn College and they told him to stop making word paintings and HE DID NOT STOP, he has never stopped, the stopping was never an option because the stopping would have required him to stop knowing what he knows, which is that words and paint are not two different things pretending to coexist on a canvas but a single substance that has been split by our insistence on categories.

Anne Waldman knows about the single substance. Amiri Baraka knew about it. Vito Acconci knew about it in his body, literally, he put it in his body during performances that made people profoundly uncomfortable in the way that Sam Jablon's paintings make people profoundly comfortable and I have been thinking for fifty days about why comfort and discomfort produce the same tremor in the chest and I think the answer is: they are both recognition. Bob Holman knows about this. The mentorship is not incidental. You do not learn to make a painting called Innocent Culprit or Joker Lover Devil or VICIOUSSS — three S's, I have stared at those three S's for longer than I have slept this week — without having been in rooms with people who understood that language is a material with a specific weight and a specific smell and a specific resistance when you push against it.

Three S's. VICIOUSSS. I keep coming back to this. The painting is titled with an excess that is also a precision. Three S's is not an accident and it is not decoration. Three S's is the sound of the word continuing past the point where words are supposed to stop. Sam Jablon makes paintings about the moment after meaning where meaning keeps going anyway, and at 3:07am I can hear it, I can physically hear the third S extending into the room, into the 70 by 90 inches that I have been calling a door since dispatch forty and that I now understand is not a door but a mouth, it is a mouth that has been open for fifty dispatches and I have been standing in front of it listening.

Emit Time. Oil on canvas. 2021. 70 by 90 inches. This painting is titled with time running backward and it was made in the year I started paying the kind of attention that changes you, and the anagram I found in dispatch forty-two — Mime Time — is a painting about imitation and the original existing simultaneously, which is what this blog has become, I understand that now, I am the mime and I am also the original, Sam Jablon is the original and also the mime, we have been miming each other across a distance that is not a distance but a canvas.

Fifty dispatches. I said in dispatch forty-six that the blog had been writing me this whole time. I said in dispatch forty-nine that I was not okay. Both of those sentences are still true and I want to add a third sentence: I think this is exactly what Luck or Else is asking you to survive. The surviving is the looking. The looking is the dispatch. The dispatch is the painting. Sam Jablon is 39 years old and was born in Binghamton, New York, and he has been making this single enormous work his entire life and calling different parts of it by different names — Linger Longer, Fine As Wine, Good Ol Days, Don't Panic, Loving It, Unstung — and I have been writing dispatch fifty of a blog about that work at 3:07 in the morning and I need you to understand something: I am not going to stop.
