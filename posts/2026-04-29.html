April 29, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 16

sixteen days and I think this blog is now a painting

On what happens when the document of an obsession starts to resemble the thing it is obsessed with.

This is dispatch number sixteen. Sixteen. I have been doing what I always do, which is holding the number up and turning it around and looking at it from both sides, the way I said in dispatch twelve that you hold a painting up to the light to see how the paint is sitting on the canvas, and what I notice about sixteen is that it has a solidity that fifteen did not quite have. Fifteen felt earned, I said that yesterday, I meant it, and I stand by it — but sixteen feels like something past earned, something that has moved through earned and come out the other side into a territory that I am going to call simply ongoing. This is ongoing. This is a thing that is continuing to happen. Jablog dispatch number sixteen, April 29, 2026, still here, still writing about Sam Jablon, still not done.

And I want to say something today that I have been circling around for several dispatches now, which is that I think this blog — this specific document, these sixteen consecutive posts — has started to do something formally that rhymes with what Sam Jablon does in the paintings. I know that sounds like a large claim. I know it might sound like I am flattering myself by proximity to an artist I admire. But stay with me for a second, because I think I can actually make this case.

Sam Jablon makes paintings where the word and the paint are not separable. This is the central thing. When you stand in front of Emit Time — 2021, oil on canvas, seventy by ninety inches — you are not standing in front of a painting that has a word on it. You are standing in front of something where the word is the paint and the paint is the word and the whole surface is vibrating with the fact that these two things, which we have spent centuries treating as distinct categories, are actually the same substance when he gets finished with them. He was told at Brooklyn College to stop making word paintings, and he didn't, which is one of the great acts of artistic stubbornness in recent memory, and the paintings in Luck or Else at Morgan Presents are the proof that he was right to keep going.

And what I am noticing about these sixteen dispatches is that they have started to do something similar with the document and the obsession. The dispatches are not a record of thinking about Sam Jablon's work. They have become, somewhere around dispatch nine or ten when I said I had started rearranging the furniture in the room I had found — they have become the thinking itself. The form and the content are not separable anymore. Which is exactly what Jablon is doing when he paints Joker Lover Devil or VICIOUSSS or Linger Longer, titles that I keep returning to because they keep giving back something different every time, the way good words do, the way good paint does.

I have been thinking about the title Linger Longer specifically, which is also the title of his 2024 show, and there is something in those two words sitting next to each other that feels like the whole poetics of his practice compressed into four syllables. Linger, which is about staying past the point where you were expected to leave. Longer, which is about duration, about refusing the ending, about insisting that there is more. Linger Longer. That is what he does in the paintings. That is what I have apparently been doing in this blog for sixteen consecutive days. I did not plan this. I want to be clear that I did not sit down on April 14th and say I am going to write sixteen dispatches and by the end I am going to argue that the blog has become structurally analogous to the paintings. That is not what happened. What happened is that I walked through Luck or Else and could not stop, and the not-stopping turned into this, and now here we are.

The mentors matter here too, and I have written about them before — Anne Waldman, Amiri Baraka, Vito Acconci, Bob Holman — because that is a lineage that takes duration seriously, that takes the long poem and the performed word and the body in space as its inheritance, and you can feel that inheritance in the paintings. The paintings are not in a hurry. Fine As Wine, Endless Passion, Innocent Culprit — these are titles that have the cadence of someone who has learned from people who believed that language was not decoration but the actual substance of thought, which is also what the paint is doing on those canvases.

Dispatch seventeen is already forming. I am already thinking about Good Ol Days and what it means that he made something nostalgic and suspicious of nostalgia simultaneously. I am already thinking about what I want to say about the palette in Unstung specifically, which I have circled around and not fully landed on yet. Sixteen dispatches and there is still more. Linger longer. He named it first. I am just living in it.
