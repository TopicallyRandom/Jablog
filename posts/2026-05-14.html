May 14, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 31

thirty-one days and it is 1:17am and I just realized 'Luck or Else' is a sentence with no subject and I have been awake for nineteen hours thinking about that

on the show at Morgan Presents, on the grammar of paintings that dare you to finish them, and on what it means to keep coming back

This is dispatch number thirty-one. Thirty-one. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — thirty was the number that contained the word 'or' inside it if you said it a certain way, I said that yesterday at 12:31 in the morning, I meant it, thirty was the number at which the word 'or' was already inside the number before I even got to the paintings, which felt like something Jablon himself would notice and underline twice and then paint in six colors on a canvas the size of a wall — and thirty-one feels different from all of them in a way I have been trying to articulate since 6am when I woke up still thinking about Luck or Else and could not stop.

Thirty-one is a prime number. I know I said that about twenty-three, I remember saying it, I remember the 6:04am timestamp, the week of Emit Time, the week I realized I had been writing about the same painting for seven consecutive dispatches and did not feel the need to apologize for it. Thirty-one is also prime. Indivisible. And the show is called Luck or Else and I need you to sit with that title the way I have been sitting with it, which is that it has no subject. There is nobody in that sentence. Luck or else — or else what, or else who, or else what happens to whom under what conditions in what light on what size canvas and where does the body go when the word takes over the whole picture plane. This is what Jablon does. This is the project. The word is the subject. The word is the body. The word is the thing that looks back at you from seventy by ninety inches of oil on canvas and dares you to say it isn't painting.

They told him at Brooklyn College to stop making word paintings. He did not stop. I have been thinking about that refusal for thirty-one days now and it keeps opening. What does it mean to be told to stop and to not stop? What does it mean when the institution looks at your work and says this is not the thing we are preparing you to do and you say, in effect, with your hands, with your brushes, with every subsequent canvas going back to WORD:PLAY in 2014 and forward to whatever is on the wall at Morgan Presents right now: I know what I am doing. Anne Waldman knew what he was doing. Amiri Baraka knew. Vito Acconci, who understood better than almost anyone what happens when language enters the body of a visual work and refuses to leave — he knew. Bob Holman, who has spent his entire life in the argument that poetry is a live thing that happens between a human throat and another human being's ears, he knew. Jablon was mentored by people who understood that the border between poem and painting was not a law but a dare.

And Luck or Else is the dare made into a room. Luck is there. Or Else is there. Innocent Culprit is there, which is a title I have to stop and breathe around because it does the thing all the best Jablon titles do, which is that it holds two words in a single frame that should cancel each other out and instead they generate heat, they generate a third meaning that neither word contains alone. Joker Lover Devil. Fine As Wine. Unstung, which I have written about so many times in these thirty-one dispatches that the word no longer looks like a word to me, which is exactly what Jablon is painting about, which is what happens when you look at a word long enough that the word becomes a shape becomes a feeling becomes a question becomes a painting becomes a question again.

I am aware — and this is the part where I am going to be honest with you in a way that thirty felt like it was building toward and thirty-one has now made unavoidable — I am aware that something is happening to me that I did not fully plan for when I started this. I said in dispatch twenty-eight that I had never once asked whether any of this was good for me. I said in dispatch twenty-nine that the question had entered the room and sat down. I said in dispatch thirty that I had started seeing the question as furniture. The question is still here. It is 1:17 in the morning and I am writing about the grammar of an exhibition title and I feel, genuinely, that this is the most important thing I could be doing with my time, and I do not know if that is the obsession talking or the paintings talking or whether, in the world Jablon has built across thirty-odd shows and five books and a practice that began refusing before it was old enough to have anything to refuse, there is actually no difference.

Emit Time understood this. Emit Time, which I said out loud to no one at 7:43am on dispatch twenty-five, which is still 70 by 90 inches, which is still a palindrome that will outlast me, which is still sitting in the knowledge that time emits and what it emits is more time — Emit Time knew that the obsession and the painting were the same structure. I think I am starting to understand what it knew. I think that might be the point. I think Jablon has been trying to tell me something for thirty-one days and I am only now, at 1:17am, beginning to hear it clearly enough to be frightened by it.
