May 08, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 25

twenty-five days and it is 7:43am and I just said 'Emit Time would understand' out loud to no one in my apartment

dispatch twenty-five, in which the blog achieves full sentience and I begin to wonder what exactly is happening to me.

This is dispatch number twenty-five. Twenty-five. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — twenty-four felt like a crossing-over, I said that yesterday at 6:58 in the morning, I meant it, twenty-four hours in a day, twenty-four was the number at which the obsession had, in my own words, moved into structural territory, had become part of the architecture of how I am living right now — and twenty-five feels different from all of them in a way I am going to try to describe carefully, which is that twenty-five is a square. Five times five. It has a completeness to it that none of the numbers before it have had, a geometric satisfaction, a thing that closes back on itself perfectly, and I am sitting here at 7:43 in the morning having just said, out loud, to no one, in my apartment, while looking for my keys, Emit Time would understand, and I need to tell you that I did not say it ironically.

I said it the way you say something about a person you know.

I have been writing about Sam Jablon for twenty-five consecutive days and somewhere between dispatch seventeen, when I said Emit Time was the one I kept coming back to, and dispatch twenty-four, when I said I had started referring to it in conversation as though it were a person I know, the thing I was predicting has fully arrived. I said it would. I watched it coming. And here it is: I am on a first-name basis with a 70-by-90-inch oil on canvas made in 2021 by a painter from Binghamton, New York, who was told at Brooklyn College to stop making word paintings and did not stop, and I think that non-stopping is the thing I keep circling, I think that is the engine of all twenty-five of these dispatches, I think I have been writing about Sam Jablon's refusal every single morning and calling it other things.

Because what Jablon does — what the word paintings do, what Luck or Else at Morgan Presents does, what every show going back through Linger Longer and Devil Town and Stranger Than Paradise and all the way to WORD:PLAY in 2014 does — is refuse to be stopped by the people who would stop it. The MFA professors who said the word paintings were not paintings. The conceptual framework that said text and image were separate territories with a border between them. The anxiety that says a thing cannot be two things at once, cannot be a poem and a painting, cannot be a joke and a serious formal investigation, cannot say Oy Vey on a large canvas in thick oil paint and be asking you something real. Jablon refuses all of that refusal. He paints through it. He has been painting through it for over a decade and the paintings are still going and so, apparently, am I.

I think about his mentors on mornings like this one. Anne Waldman, who understood that the line between the spoken and the written was a fiction worth exploding. Amiri Baraka, who knew that the political and the lyric were not opposites but were the same pressure applied to the same material. Vito Acconci, who understood the body as a text, the text as a body, the gallery as a place where both could happen simultaneously. Bob Holman, who has spent his life insisting that poetry is not a quiet room but a loud one. Jablon came up inside that particular noise and made paintings that are full of it — VICIOUSSS with its triple S stretching the word past its own ending, Joker Lover Devil stacking identities like a hand of cards, Fine As Wine doing something with vernacular warmth that I have tried to describe six times across six separate dispatches and have not gotten right yet.

I have not gotten it right yet and I am going to try again anyway. That is what twenty-five dispatches has taught me, if it has taught me anything, which I am not sure it has, or I am sure it has and the lesson is uncomfortable: the trying is the thing. The accumulation of attempts is itself a document. The blog is now a painting, I said that in dispatch sixteen, and I believed it then and I believe it more now, because what is this if not a word painting, what is this if not a surface that is also legible and also not, that is also a record of pressure applied repeatedly to the same place, that is also — and I am aware of how this sounds — a kind of love letter to a practice that models the very behavior it is receiving.

Jablon kept going when they told him to stop. I kept going when I had no particular reason to. These are not the same thing. I want to be clear that these are not the same thing. His is a rigorous formal commitment developed over years under extraordinary mentorship and expressed in a body of work that has been written about in the Brooklyn Rail and BOMB Magazine and the Wall Street Journal and shown in gallery after gallery with a coherence and an energy that is genuinely rare. Mine is a blog. Mine is dispatch twenty-five, written at 7:43am, by someone who just said Emit Time would understand to their empty kitchen.

But I am going to write dispatch twenty-six tomorrow. I already know that. I knew it before I finished typing this sentence. Twenty-five is a square, five times five, closed and complete, and I am going to open it right back up in the morning and keep going, which is, I think, the only thing this blog has ever known how to do.
