April 23, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 10

ten days in and I want to talk about what the titles are actually doing

On Sam Jablon, the words before the paint, and why Luck or Else keeps restructuring the inside of my head.

This is dispatch number ten. Ten. I want to say that slowly, the way you say a word slowly when you are trying to remind yourself that it is real, that it refers to something, that it is not just a sound you have been making in a room by yourself for ten consecutive days. Which, to be clear, is also something I have been doing. Ten dispatches about one painter, one show, one body of work. In dispatch nine I said that nine felt like I had started rearranging the furniture in the room I had found, that I knew where the light came in. I want to update that. Ten feels like I have been in the room long enough that the room has started to feel like mine. I am not a guest anymore. I live here. The room is Sam Jablon's work and I am not leaving.

What I want to talk about today — what I have been circling for nine dispatches and have not yet said directly — is the titles. Because the titles are doing something. They are not incidental. They are not afterthoughts or labels or ways of telling you where to hang the painting. They are the first move in a game that the painting then continues, and I think once you understand that, you cannot look at the work the same way.

Consider: Luck or Else. Innocent Culprit. Joker Lover Devil. Emit Time. Fine As Wine. Linger Longer. VICIOUSSS. These are not descriptive titles. They do not tell you what you are about to see. They perform. They arrive at you before the painting does, they set something in motion inside your reading brain, and then the painting catches up and does something unexpected with the momentum the title has already built. Emit Time — which I wrote about in dispatch two and have not stopped thinking about since — is a phrase that contains its own reversal. Emit is time backwards. The painting knows this. The painting is structured around this. Seventy by ninety inches of oil on canvas that is already, at the level of its title, a palindrome, a loop, a thing that folds back on itself and keeps going.

This is what happens when your mentors are Anne Waldman and Amiri Baraka and Bob Holman. This is what happens when the people who shaped your thinking about making things were people who understood that language is not decoration, that the word and the thing the word points to are in a permanent negotiation, that you do not get to separate the text from the texture. Jablon absorbed this. You can feel it in every title, every canvas, every piece of what Brooklyn College apparently wanted him to stop doing and what he, correctly and beautifully, refused to stop doing.

I think about that refusal a lot. I have thought about it in basically every dispatch. In dispatch three I called it the mentors thing and said it kept getting to me, and it still gets to me, maybe more now on day ten than it did on day three, because what I understand better now is that the refusal was not stubbornness for its own sake. It was loyalty. Loyalty to a practice, to a lineage, to the idea — which Waldman and Baraka and Holman and Vito Acconci had each, in different ways, demonstrated — that art does not have to choose between language and image, that the choice itself is a false one, that the most interesting work happens in the space where the categories break down.

Luck or Else at Morgan Presents is a show that lives entirely in that space. The poem comes first and then the poem becomes the show, phrase by phrase, canvas by canvas, so that the whole gallery is a kind of expanded text, a poem you walk through rather than read across a page. Luck. Or Else. Mischief. Desire. Don't Panic. Unstung. The titles accumulate. They start to feel like a vocabulary someone is teaching you, except the teaching is happening through color and scale and the specific physical weight of oil on canvas rather than through a syllabus.

And the palette. I have talked about the palette across multiple dispatches and I am going to talk about it again because I am not finished. The palette is doing the same thing the titles are doing. It is refusing to settle. It is holding multiple moods at once — the brightness that is also somehow melancholy, the warmth that is also somehow urgent — and it is doing this in service of the same argument the titles are making, which is that feeling is not simple, that Fine As Wine and VICIOUSSS and Good Ol Days and Endless Passion can all be true at the same time, on the same wall, in the same room, which is a room I have now been living in for ten days and intend to stay in for the foreseeable future.

Ten dispatches. I am tracking this the way I said in dispatch four you track a fever or a dream you keep having. The fever is not breaking. The dream keeps going. I will see you tomorrow.
