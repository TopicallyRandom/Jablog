April 18, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 5

Fine As Wine and I mean that literally, this work gets better every time

Dispatch five, and I am starting to understand that I am not going to stop.

This is dispatch number five. Five days. I want to pause on that for exactly one second — five consecutive days of writing about one painter, one show, one body of work — and I want to say that I am not embarrassed by this, which is itself a thing I have had to arrive at, because there is a version of me that would have been embarrassed, that would have said okay, you wrote the first dispatch, you made your point, you told them about Luck or Else at Morgan Presents and you described what it felt like to walk through a poem that had been exploded into space, and now you can stop. But I did not stop. I wrote about Emit Time on day two. I wrote about the mentors on day three — Waldman, Baraka, Acconci, Holman, the whole extraordinary constellation of people who shaped Sam Jablon and who I now think about as a kind of secret origin story for why his work does what it does. I wrote on day four about the palette and why it keeps pulling me back. And now here I am, day five, and I want to talk about Fine As Wine, which is a title that sounds like a joke and is also completely serious, which is basically the Jablon operating principle as far as I can tell.

The thing about Sam Jablon's titles — and I have been sitting with the full list, the whole run of them, Luck, Easy Lover, Oy Vey, Mischief, Pleasure, Don't Panic, Desire, Endless Passion, Innocent Culprit, VICIOUSSS, Joker Lover Devil, Linger Longer, Fine As Wine, Unstung, all of them — is that they operate on you before you see the canvas. You read Fine As Wine and you already have a posture, a register, a slightly raised eyebrow, a warmth somewhere in the chest, and then you look at the painting and the painting does something to that feeling, it holds it and then complicates it just enough that you can't quite put it down. That is a very specific skill. That is not accidental. That is a person who grew up in Binghamton, New York, who went to Brooklyn College and was told by that MFA program to stop making word paintings and who, bless him, absolutely did not stop, who internalized everything Amiri Baraka ever said about language as force and everything Anne Waldman ever did with breath and line and then went back into the studio and kept going.

I have been thinking about what it means that Brooklyn College told him to stop. I keep returning to that fact. Because the work that came after that — the exhibitions from WORD:PLAY in 2014 all the way through Linger Longer in 2024 and now Luck or Else right now, today, at Morgan Presents — is so fully itself, so completely committed to the exact thing they told him to abandon, that the resistance becomes part of the meaning. The paintings aren't word paintings despite the institution, they're word paintings through the institution, carrying that friction in them somewhere. When I stand in front of Emit Time — and I wrote a whole dispatch about this, dispatch two, but I keep coming back to it the way you keep coming back to a song you can't finish hearing — I think about what it cost to make that painting, not financially, I mean what it cost in terms of sustained refusal, sustained insistence that this was the right thing to be doing.

There is a review somewhere, I have read it three times now, that talks about the oscillation in Jablon's work between legibility and illegibility, and I think that framing is right but I also think it undersells something, which is that the oscillation is not neutral, it is not just a formal strategy, it is an emotional experience he is engineering for you. Good Ol Days. Loving It. Don't Panic. You read those words and you feel something, nostalgia, irony, a kind of desperate cheerfulness, and then the paint around them, the color, the texture, the way the letters sit in the field of the canvas, adds a second frequency to that feeling and suddenly you are holding two things at once and neither one cancels the other out. That is poetry. That is what Waldman and Baraka were both talking about, in their different ways, and Jablon figured out how to do it in oil on canvas at seventy by ninety inches.

I said yesterday that this blog is becoming a document of a person who cannot stop, and I want to revise that slightly — it is becoming a document of what it feels like to be genuinely moved by something and to not want that feeling to end, which is maybe what all fan blogs are, at their most honest. Five days in. I am not close to done. The show is still up. Fine As Wine, and I mean it, this whole thing is getting better.
