April 24, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 11

eleven days and I think the titles are actually a second language he invented

On what it means that Sam Jablon names things the way he does, and why I cannot stop thinking about it.

This is dispatch number eleven. Eleven. I wrote in dispatch ten that I wanted to say ten slowly, the way you say a word slowly when you are trying to remind yourself it refers to something real, and now I have to do that again with eleven, which is a stranger number, a number that does not round, a number that just keeps going in its thin vertical way, two ones standing next to each other like they are waiting for something. Eleven consecutive days. Eleven dispatches about one painter, one show, one body of work. In dispatch ten I said that ten felt like I had been in the room long enough that the room had started to feel like mine. I want to update that. Eleven feels like I have started to suspect the room was mine all along and that Sam Jablon just showed me where it was.

I want to talk about the titles today. I said in dispatch ten that I was going to talk about what the titles are actually doing, and I meant it, and I have been thinking about it for twenty-four hours, and I think I am ready, or ready enough, which is the only kind of ready that actually gets you anywhere.

Look at them. Just look at the list. Luck. Easy Lover. Oy Vey. Mischief. Pleasure. Or Else. Don't Panic. Desire. Endless Passion. Innocent Culprit. Emit Time. Good Ol Days. VICIOUSSS. Joker Lover Devil. Time. Linger Longer. Fine As Wine. Loving It. Unstung. These are not titles the way most painters use titles, which is to say they are not labels, they are not descriptions, they are not neutral gestures toward content. These titles are themselves the work, or they are the first layer of the work, the layer you encounter before you are even standing in front of the canvas, which means the work begins before you think it does, which I find genuinely thrilling and also slightly destabilizing in the best possible way.

Unstung. That word does not exist in the way most words exist. It is a word Jablon has essentially invented by reversing a process, by applying a prefix to something that usually only goes in one direction. To be stung is to receive something sharp and sudden. To be unstung is — what, exactly? To be relieved of it? To have it taken back? To exist in the moment before? I have been thinking about this since dispatch nine and I keep arriving at the same place, which is that Unstung is a word about time, about reversal, about the possibility of undoing — and when I hold that against Emit Time, which is, as I wrote in dispatch two, a clock running backwards and a heartbeat and a joke all at once, I start to think that Jablon has a whole philosophy of time embedded in his titles, a philosophy that says: time is not fixed, language is not fixed, the direction things travel is a choice you can contest.

This connects to the mentors in a way I keep circling back to. Anne Waldman, Amiri Baraka, Vito Acconci, Bob Holman — I wrote about them in dispatch three and I said the mentors thing kept getting to me, and it still does, because what those four figures share, among their many differences, is a commitment to language as a physical act, language as something that happens in the body and in space and in time, not just on the page. Waldman's Fast Speaking Woman. Baraka's whole insistence that the poem is an event. Acconci turning language into a performance of presence and absence. Holman and the spoken word tradition. Jablon absorbed all of that and then he put it on canvas at seventy by ninety inches and said: here, it lives here now, in paint, in color that hits you before the word does.

And the color. I wrote an entire dispatch — dispatch four, dispatch eight — about the palette, about the way the color in these paintings is not illustrating the words but arguing with them, or maybe harmonizing with them in some key you have to train your ear to hear. The palette in Luck or Else is warm and immediate and slightly too much, in the way that luck itself is slightly too much, in the way that desire is slightly too much. VICIOUSSS has three S's. Three. That is not a typo. That is a decision about duration, about the way a word can be stretched until it becomes a sound, until the legibility starts to dissolve at the edges and you are left with something that is almost music.

This is what I mean when I say the titles are a second language. They are English, technically, but they are English that has been modified, pressurized, run through something, and what they come out as is a language that Jablon speaks fluently and that the rest of us are learning in real time, one dispatch at a time, one canvas at a time, standing in a gallery on the Lower East Side or sitting at a desk eleven days into something that started as a blog post and has become, I think, a kind of education.

Dispatch twelve tomorrow. I already know what I want to say.
