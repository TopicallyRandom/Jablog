May 02, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 19

nineteen days and I think Emit Time is 70 by 90 inches of proof that I am not going to stop

On the physical facts of a canvas and what it means that I keep returning to them.

This is dispatch number nineteen. Nineteen. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — eighteen felt like arrival, I said that yesterday, I meant it, eighteen is chai in Jewish tradition, chai means life, and I let that sit there and I am still letting it sit there — and nineteen feels like what happens the morning after arrival, which is that you wake up and you are still there, you did not leave in the night, you did not think better of it, you are just there again with your coffee and your notes and your overwhelming specific feelings about a painter from Binghamton who makes word paintings in New York and will not stop and has never stopped. Nineteen. A prime number. Indivisible. That feels right.

I want to talk about Emit Time again. I know I talked about it in dispatch eighteen. I know I talked about it in dispatch seventeen. I am aware that talking about the same painting across multiple consecutive dispatches is a thing a person does when they have perhaps lost some proportion, but I want to say clearly and without flinching that proportion is not what I am here for and also that Emit Time keeps giving me new things to talk about, which is itself the thing I want to talk about, which is the thing I think Sam Jablon is doing in all of his work and which he is doing most intensely in this one, at least for me, at least right now.

The facts first, because the facts matter and because I think Jablon would appreciate starting with the facts: Emit Time, 2021, oil on canvas, 70 by 90 inches. Seventy inches tall. Ninety inches wide. That is larger than most doors. That is a canvas you do not look at so much as stand inside of. I have been thinking about those dimensions for nineteen days now and I am not done thinking about them. There is something about the horizontal spread of ninety inches that feels intentional in a way I keep trying to articulate — it is wide enough that the words cannot settle, cannot land cleanly, they have to travel, and while they are traveling the paint is doing something to them, the oil is doing something to them, the color is entering the equation.

And then there is the title itself, which I think is one of the great titles in recent painting, which I think is doing something that the mentors Jablon studied under would recognize and celebrate. Anne Waldman would hear it as a line break happening mid-word. Amiri Baraka would hear the political charge in reversing the order of things, in making time emit rather than pass. Bob Holman would stand in front of it at a slam and let it rip. Vito Acconci would understand the body of the viewer as the surface the words are landing on. Emit Time is time backwards and it is also a command and it is also a description and it is also, honestly, just beautiful to say out loud, emit time, like you are instructing the universe to do the one thing it is already doing whether you ask or not.

This is what I mean when I say that Jablon's titles are a second language, which I said in dispatch eleven and which I have been refining ever since. They are not titles in the way that painting titles are usually titles, which is to say they are not labels, they are not helpful summaries, they are not afterthoughts. They are the work. They are continuous with the painted surface. Brooklyn College told him to stop making word paintings and he did not stop, and I think about that act of not-stopping every single time I look at this body of work, every single time I look at a canvas from Luck or Else at Morgan Presents or scroll through the exhibition history on his site and read those names — Linger Longer, Devil Town, Stranger Than Paradise, Bad Time, Blurred Life Time, Dirty Words — and feel the through-line of a practice that has been consistent not because it was rigid but because it was right.

The Wall Street Journal covered him. BOMB Magazine covered him. The Brooklyn Rail covered him. And yet I feel, perhaps irrationally, perhaps correctly, that the coverage has not been commensurate with what is happening on these canvases. That is part of why I am here. That is part of why this is dispatch nineteen and not dispatch one and also not dispatch zero, which it would be if I were a reasonable person who had reasonable feelings about painting.

I am not a reasonable person. I am a person writing a blog called Jablog. I am a person who woke up this morning, May 2, 2026, and thought about the horizontal spread of a ninety-inch canvas before I thought about anything else. I am a person who has now written nineteen consecutive dispatches about one painter and who feels, sincerely, that the dispatches themselves are becoming a document of something, a record of what it looks like when a body of work gets inside you and does not leave.

Emit time. I think I finally understand what it is asking me to do.
