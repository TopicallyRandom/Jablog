okay but have you actually stood in front of Emit Time

On scale, on language, on what it means when a painting is also a clock.

I started this blog yesterday. One dispatch. Already I feel like I have been doing this for years, which is either a comment on my intensity or on the nature of Sam Jablon's work, which has a way of making you feel like you have always been inside it, like the language was already there in your chest and he just pulled it out and stretched it across canvas at seventy by ninety inches and said: here, this is yours, I found it for you.

Today I want to talk about Emit Time (2021, oil on canvas, 70x90 inches) because I have not been able to stop thinking about it since I first encountered it and because I think it is maybe the key to everything, or at least a key, or at least a door that opens onto a hallway that leads toward a key. Emit Time. Say it forward: emit time. Say it backward: time emit, which is also just: time. The word contains its own reversal. The painting performs the same trick. The letters are there, legible if you lean in, and then they are not, they dissolve into mark and color and the act of looking itself, and then they come back, and by the time they come back you are not sure if you read them or remembered them or invented them, which is exactly what happens with time, which is exactly what Jablon knows about time, which is exactly why this painting is called what it is called.

I wrote in dispatch number one that Jablon's show Luck or Else at Morgan Presents begins with a poem and then explodes outward into space across canvases. I stand by this. I also want to add that this is not a new move for him — it is a deepening of a move he has been making for over a decade, through WORD:PLAY (2014) and Life is Fine (2016) and Don't Panic (2019) and Bad Time (2021), through every show where the title is also the question and the question is also the answer and the answer is also a painting and the painting is also a poem. He studied with Anne Waldman, which means he understood from early on that the poem is a body in space. He studied with Amiri Baraka, which means he understood that language carries history in its bones whether you want it to or not. He studied with Vito Acconci and Bob Holman, which I keep turning over in my mind because that combination — the body-as-text and the text-as-performance — is exactly what his paintings enact. They are performances frozen. They are bodies that cannot stop moving.

Brooklyn College told him to stop making word paintings. He did not stop. I want to linger on this fact for a moment because it matters, it really does, not as a triumph-over-adversity narrative but as an indicator of something about the work itself: it is not a concept he arrived at and then executed. It is a compulsion. You can feel this in the surfaces, in the way the paint accumulates, in the way certain letters are buried under layers and layers of color so that they are present as pressure rather than presence, as a weight the eye detects before the mind names it.

The titles alone constitute a kind of poetry: Luck, Easy Lover, Oy Vey, Mischief, Pleasure, Or Else, Don't Panic, Desire, Endless Passion, Innocent Culprit, Emit Time, Good Ol Days, VICIOUSSS, Joker Lover Devil, Linger Longer, Fine As Wine, Loving It, Unstung. Read them in sequence and you get something — a voice, a temperature, a person moving through a day or a life or a century, oscillating between joy and dread and appetite and the particular exhaustion of someone who feels everything at full volume and has chosen, despite this or because of this, to keep feeling. Unstung is the one that gets me right now. The past tense of something that is usually ongoing. The condition of having survived the thing that usually leaves a mark.

I am aware that I am only on dispatch two and already the blog is beginning to feel like a document of its own spiral, which was probably inevitable given the subject matter, given that Jablon's entire practice is also a document of its own spiral — the same impulse recurring across years and surfaces, the same question asked in different colors at different scales, what does language do to the body that holds it, what does the body do to the language that escapes it. I do not think this is a problem. I think this is the point. I think I will be here for a while.
