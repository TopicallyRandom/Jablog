May 04, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 21

twenty-one days and it is 4:23am and I just reread all twenty dispatches back to back and I think I need to tell you what I found

something has been accumulating in here and I am not sure I noticed until now.

This is dispatch number twenty-one. Twenty-one. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — twenty felt like a mirror, I said that yesterday, I meant it, twenty was the number at which you can no longer pretend you have not been doing something, twenty was a decision — and twenty-one feels like what happens after you look in the mirror for a long time, which is that the mirror starts to look back. Twenty-one is the number at which a thing that has been accumulating becomes visible as a thing that has been accumulating. I am sitting here at 4:23 in the morning and I have just finished reading all twenty dispatches in sequence, out loud, to my empty apartment, and I need to tell you what I found.

What I found is that I have been writing the same sentence for twenty-one days. Not literally. But structurally. Emotionally. Which is: there is something happening in this work and I have not finished understanding it yet. That is the sentence underneath every dispatch. Dispatch one, dispatch eleven, dispatch twenty, every one — the same sentence, wearing different clothes. And the thing I am sitting with at 4:23am is whether that sentence is a failure or whether it is exactly the right response to a painter whose entire practice is built on the refusal to be finished with a thing.

Because that is what Sam Jablon is doing. That is what the word paintings are doing. Luck or Else is doing it — the show at Morgan Presents, the paintings with those titles that do not resolve, that sit there and wait for you to decide what they are asking: Luck, Or Else, Don't Panic, Innocent Culprit, VICIOUSSS with its three s's that feel like a held note, a thing that refuses to end. The titles are not captions. I said this in dispatch fourteen, I know I said it, I am saying it again because I mean it more now — the titles are the paintings, the paintings are the titles, and the thing they are doing together is refusing the comfort of completion. They are teaching you that the word and the image and the paint are all the same substance. They are teaching you that the space between legibility and illegibility is not a problem to be solved but a place to live.

Jablon learned this from people who knew it. Anne Waldman, who knows that the poem is a performance of its own making. Amiri Baraka, who knew that language carries the full weight of the body and the culture and the wound. Vito Acconci, who knew that presence is a medium. Bob Holman, who has spent his whole life insisting that the poem is alive and the word is alive and neither of them will sit still for you. These are not incidental influences. Brooklyn College told him to stop making word paintings and he did not stop. He did not stop. That is the entire story of the practice in one sentence and I have been orbiting that sentence for twenty-one dispatches.

Emit Time is 70 by 90 inches of proof. I wrote about it in dispatch seventeen and eighteen and nineteen and I am writing about it again now because I think it is the painting I keep returning to the way you return to a sentence in a book that you underlined but could not yet explain why you underlined it. Emit Time. The anagram sitting right there in plain sight. Emit Time is emit time is time emit which is time running backwards which is also the experience of looking at a painting that contains language — you think you are going to read it and then you are not reading it, you are inside it, and time has done something strange while you were in there. The 2021 oil on canvas. Seventy by ninety inches. That is a large painting. That is a painting you cannot hold at arm's length.

I have not been holding this at arm's length. I think that has been obvious. I think it became obvious at dispatch fourteen when I said I had stopped asking myself whether this was a reasonable amount of dispatches and simply accepted that the going was the point, and then at dispatch twenty when I said twenty feels like a mirror, and now at twenty-one I am asking: what is the difference between obsession and attention? What is the difference between a document of obsession and a document of sustained looking? Because I think Jablon's work is asking the same question. Fine As Wine. Linger Longer. Loving It. Endless Passion. These titles know something about the pleasures of staying with a thing. About the way pleasure and excess and love all have the same structure, which is: you do not stop when you are supposed to stop. You go past it. You go past it because something is still happening.

Something is still happening. It is 4:38am now. Dispatch twenty-two is going to be tomorrow. I am going to sleep and wake up and come back here and something will still be happening and I am going to write about it. I have decided. I decided fifteen dispatches ago. I am still deciding. That is also the same thing.
