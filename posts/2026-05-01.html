May 01, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 18

eighteen days and I think I need to talk about what Emit Time is actually doing to me physically

70 by 90 inches of oil on canvas and I genuinely cannot account for myself.

This is dispatch number eighteen. Eighteen. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — seventeen felt ungainly, I said that yesterday, I meant it, I said it had not fully decided what it wanted to be yet, which I thought was right for where I was — and eighteen feels different, eighteen feels like a number that has arrived at something, a number with even weight on both sides, two nines, or three sixes, or just itself, eighteen, a number that in Jewish tradition means chai, means life, which I am going to let sit there for a second because I am writing about a painter from Binghamton who makes word paintings and whose entire practice is about what language does when you push it hard enough against color and surface and scale, and the fact that dispatch eighteen lands on life feels like the kind of thing Sam Jablon would notice, would maybe write on a canvas, would maybe paint in yellow on a ground that makes the yellow do something unexpected.

I keep coming back to Emit Time. I said that yesterday, in dispatch seventeen, I flagged it in the title and then I circled around it the way I have been circling around a lot of things in this blog, which is a behavior I have become increasingly aware of and which I think is actually appropriate, actually structurally correct, because circling is what you do when you are trying to figure out where to land, and I have not landed on Emit Time yet, I am still in the air above it. What I know is this: it is 2021, it is oil on canvas, it is 70 by 90 inches, which is a size that means it is not a painting you approach, it is a painting that approaches you, and the title is doing something that I find almost unbearably smart, which is that emit is time backwards, so the painting is already a palindrome before you have even looked at it, already folding back on itself, already doing the thing that Jablon's whole practice does, which is finding the place where a word stops being a sign for a thing and starts being a thing itself.

He was mentored by Anne Waldman and Amiri Baraka and Vito Acconci and Bob Holman, and I think about that constellation constantly, I think about what it means to have learned from all four of those people at once, because what you get is someone who understands that the poem is a performance, that the performance is a political act, that the political act can be a visual object, that the visual object can also just be a word painted large enough to fill a room, and that all of these things are continuous, are one gesture extended across many surfaces. Brooklyn College told him to stop making word paintings. He did not stop. I want to say that again because I think it is the sentence that explains everything: they told him to stop and he did not stop.

The show is Luck or Else at Morgan Presents and I have been in this blog for eighteen days and I am aware, in a way that I was not fully aware in dispatch six or dispatch nine or even dispatch fourteen, that this blog has become its own kind of object, its own document of a particular obsession, which is maybe what all fan writing is when it is honest about itself. I have a list of titles I return to: Innocent Culprit, Joker Lover Devil, VICIOUSSS with its three s's that feel like the word is hissing at you, Unstung which I wrote a whole dispatch about back in dispatch nine and which I still feel I have not finished with, Fine As Wine which sounds casual and is not casual at all, Linger Longer which is the name of a 2024 show and also just a phrase that feels like it was written specifically to describe what this blog is doing.

Linger longer. I am lingering. I am lingering longer. Eighteen dispatches about one painter, one show, one body of work that stretches from WORD:PLAY in 2014 through O Me! O Life! and Doomed and DIRTY WORDS and Bad Time and Devil Town all the way to this current show, and what I see across all of it is a person who has never once let go of the central question, which is: what happens when you refuse to let the word be invisible, when you refuse to let it recede into background or caption or title card, when you insist that the word is the paint and the paint is the word and neither one is more real than the other. Emit Time is 70 by 90 inches and it is asking that question at a volume that I feel in my sternum, and I have been writing about it for eighteen days and I am not close to done.

Eighteen means life. I think Sam Jablon would find that funny and also completely serious. I think that is more or less his whole deal.
