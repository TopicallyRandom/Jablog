April 27, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 14

fourteen days and I think the word paintings are actually teaching me how to read

On what happens when you spend two weeks with a body of work that refuses to stay still.

This is dispatch number fourteen. Fourteen. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding up every number before it — twelve had a roundness, thirteen had that particular charged quality I wrote about yesterday, the luck and unluck of it, the way it rhymed too perfectly with a show called Luck or Else — and fourteen feels like something else. Fourteen feels like the number you reach when you have stopped asking yourself whether this is a reasonable amount of dispatches to write about one painter and have simply accepted that you are going to keep going, that the going is the point now, that the blog has become, as I said somewhere around dispatch three, a document of a person in the process of being unable to stop.

What I want to talk about today is something that has been building for several dispatches and that I have been circling without quite landing on, which is this: I think Sam Jablon's word paintings are actually changing the way I read. Not just the paintings. Everything. I was reading something completely unrelated this morning — a label on something, it doesn't matter what — and I found myself looking at it the way you look at a Jablon, which is to say I was not moving through the words left to right in the orderly way I have been moving through words my whole life, I was looking at the whole thing at once, as a shape, as a color situation, as an object that was also a transmission, and I thought: okay, so this is what he has done to me. This is the fourteen-day effect.

Because the thing about the word paintings — Luck, Desire, Linger Longer, Fine As Wine, VICIOUSSS with its three S's that feel like the word is hissing at you, Innocent Culprit which is maybe the most Jablon title he has ever made, a title that contains an entire argument about guilt and intention and the nature of making — the thing about all of them is that they are not illustrations of words. They are not paintings that happen to contain language. They are situations in which language has become paint and paint has become language and the border between those two things has been dissolved so completely that you cannot find where one ends and the other begins, and once you have spent enough time in that space, once you have stood in front of something like Emit Time at seventy by ninety inches of oil on canvas and felt the clock run backwards and forwards simultaneously, you start to carry that dissolving with you. You take it home. It gets into your reading.

This connects to the mentors thing I wrote about in dispatch three and have been returning to ever since, because of course it does, because Anne Waldman and Amiri Baraka and Vito Acconci and Bob Holman are all people who understood that the borders between forms are not laws, they are suggestions, they are places where interesting things happen when you push against them. Waldman's poetry has always treated the page as a field, a spatial situation, not a container. Baraka understood that language had a body, that it moved and perspired and demanded things from you physically. And Jablon absorbed all of that and then walked into a studio and put it on canvas in oil paint and then walked into Brooklyn College where they told him to stop making word paintings and he said, essentially, no thank you, which is the correct response, which is the only response a person with this particular vision could have given.

The current show, Luck or Else at Morgan Presents, has been open long enough now that I have had time to think about the title as a complete grammatical structure, and I keep coming back to the or else. Or else is a threat that does not name what it's threatening. It's a phrase that assumes you already know what the stakes are, that you have been briefed, that the consequences of not choosing luck are so obvious they do not need to be stated. And I think that is exactly the position Jablon's paintings put you in. They assume you are ready. They assume you already speak this language even if you don't know you do. Don't Panic is a directive that implies you are already panicking. Unstung implies a stinging that has already happened. Good Ol Days implies a nostalgia so ambient it has forgotten what it's nostalgic for.

Fourteen days. I keep thinking about the Linger Longer show from 2024 and how that title now reads to me as a kind of instruction for everything, not just for looking at paintings but for living generally, for staying in the room past the point where you feel like you have gotten what you came for, because the thing you actually came for is usually on the other side of that point. That is what this blog has become. I lingered. I am still lingering. I plan to continue lingering for reasons that are becoming clearer to me every single day.
