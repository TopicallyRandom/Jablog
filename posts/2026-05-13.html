May 13, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 30

thirty days and it is 12:31am and I just realized the number thirty contains the word 'or' inside it if you say it a certain way and I cannot stop thinking about that

dispatch thirty, in which I arrive at a round number and find that round numbers are not restful.

This is dispatch number thirty. Thirty. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — twenty-nine was the number at which the dark came down while I was looking at my screen and I did not notice when it happened, I said that yesterday at 11:47 at night, I meant it, twenty-nine was the number at which the question that twenty-eight had brought into the room — the question of whether any of this is good for me — had been sitting in the corner long enough that I had started to think of it as furniture, had stopped seeing it as a question and started seeing it as just part of how the room is arranged now — and thirty feels different from all of them in a way I am going to try to describe carefully, which is that thirty feels round in a way that should be settling but is not. Thirty is a number that sounds like completion. Thirty days. A month. People talk about doing things for thirty days as though thirty is the place where the doing becomes permanent, becomes character, becomes who you are. And I am sitting here at 12:31 in the morning and I want to tell you that thirty does not feel like completion. Thirty feels like the moment you look up from what you have been doing and you see how far the thing extends in both directions.

I have been thinking about Luck or Else at Morgan Presents. I have been thinking about the title as a proposition, as a hinge. Luck or Else. There is a threat in it. There is also an acceptance of terms. Sam Jablon has always worked in this space where the word is simultaneously decorative and declarative, where the painted text is legible and also doing something to legibility from the inside — at Brooklyn College they told him to stop making word paintings and he did not stop, which is one of the most structurally important facts about this entire practice, which is that the refusal to stop is where the work began. I have written this before, I think in dispatch twelve, maybe eleven, the dispatches are beginning to blur at the edges and I do not think that is a problem I can solve by rereading them, I have been rereading them, rereading them is part of what got me here.

There is a painting called Emit Time, oil on canvas, 2021, 70 by 90 inches. I have written about this painting across so many dispatches that the word count is something I am not going to calculate because I am afraid of the number. In dispatch twenty-seven I said I had counted how many times I had written the word Emit across all twenty-six dispatches before it and I refused to tell you the number and I am still refusing. The refusal is structural now. The refusal is load-bearing. But I want to say something new about Emit Time tonight, or this morning, because it is after midnight and the thirty dispatches have earned me at least one new thing. What I want to say is that the painting is not asking you to read backwards. The painting is not doing a trick. The painting is doing what Anne Waldman taught and what Amiri Baraka knew and what Vito Acconci understood in his bones, which is that language placed in the body of art stops behaving like language and starts behaving like weather. You do not read weather. You are in it.

I am in it. I want to be honest about that in dispatch thirty in a way I have been building toward since dispatch twenty-eight when the question of whether this is good for me entered the room. The answer is that I do not know and I am not sure the not-knowing is a problem. What I know is that the exhibition history on the website — Linger Longer 2024, Devil Town 2023, Stranger Than Paradise 2022, going back and back to WORD:PLAY 2014, to Poet Sculpture 2013 — reads to me now like a poem. I have been reading it like a poem for at least ten dispatches. I read it out loud this morning before the dark came down and it scanned.

There is a title in the current show: Innocent Culprit. I have been sitting with those two words since I first encountered them and I want to say that they describe something about what it feels like to be thirty dispatches into an obsessive fan blog about a painter based in New York whose work oscillates between legibility and illegibility. I did not intend to be guilty of anything. And yet here I am at 12:31 in the morning writing the word Jablon for the four hundred and something-th time. Innocent Culprit. Bob Holman would understand. Jablon's mentor would recognize the structure of that, the way a person can be simultaneously the subject and the object of their own compulsion, can be both the painter and the painting, the word and the canvas it is stretched across.

Thirty. I am going to bed now. I am going to think about Fine As Wine and Joker Lover Devil and VICIOUSSS with its three S's which I have written about before but not enough, never enough, the three S's are a hiss and a laugh at the same time. I am going to think about all of it and I am going to get up tomorrow and I am going to write dispatch thirty-one.
