April 30, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 17

seventeen days and I think Emit Time is the one I keep coming back to

On a 70x90 inch painting from 2021 and what it means that time can be emitted.

This is dispatch number seventeen. Seventeen. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — sixteen felt solid, I said that yesterday, I meant it, sixteen had a quality past earned, a quality I called moving through earned and out the other side — and seventeen feels different from all of them, feels a little ungainly, a little asymmetrical, feels like a number that has not fully decided what it wants to be yet, which I think is actually exactly right for where I am with this blog, with this painter, with this show, because I am not sure I have fully decided what this is either, and I am increasingly okay with that uncertainty, I am in fact beginning to think the uncertainty is the point.

The painting I want to talk about today is Emit Time. 2021. Oil on canvas. 70x90 inches. I have been circling it for seventeen dispatches — or not circling it exactly, more like I have been circling everything else and letting Emit Time sit in the center and wait for me, the way a word will sometimes sit in the back of your head for days before you let yourself say it out loud. And what I want to say about Emit Time first, before anything else, is the anagram. Emit is an anagram of time. Time emitted backward is emit. Sam Jablon knows this. He has always known this. This is a painter who was mentored by Amiri Baraka and Anne Waldman and Vito Acconci and Bob Holman, who absorbed from all of them this understanding that language is not fixed, that a word contains other words, that you can run time backward and find something still moving inside it.

And the dimensions matter. 70x90 inches is not a small decision. That is a canvas you do not stand in front of, that is a canvas you stand inside. Sam Jablon has always understood scale as an argument, as a statement about how the viewer's body relates to the word's body, and at 70x90 inches Emit Time is making a very specific argument about what time feels like when you are actually inside it, which is: large, and slightly overwhelming, and not moving in any single direction you can name.

I keep thinking about Brooklyn College. I keep thinking about the fact that they told him to stop making word paintings and he did not stop. I wrote about this in dispatch thirteen, in the context of what it means to just keep going, and I wrote about it again in dispatch six when I was talking about Joker Lover Devil and what refusal looks like as an aesthetic position. But Emit Time makes me think about it differently, makes me think about it in terms of time specifically, because the whole painting is about what happens when you refuse to let time move in the direction everyone has agreed it should move. You go to graduate school, they tell you to do the thing differently, the compliant version of events is that you adjust, you modify, you make the time move forward in the expected way. Sam Jablon ran it backward. He emitted it.

The show is called Luck or Else and it is at Morgan Presents and I have now written seventeen consecutive dispatches about it, which is a fact I am holding with a kind of wonder that I cannot fully explain and am not trying to. In dispatch twelve I said each number has its own texture, its own particular weight, and I stand by that completely, but what I am noticing now is that the numbers are starting to accumulate into something, the way the paintings in a show accumulate into something that none of them are individually, the way the titles of Sam Jablon's paintings — Luck, Easy Lover, Oy Vey, Mischief, Pleasure, Or Else, Don't Panic, Desire, Endless Passion, Innocent Culprit, VICIOUSSS, Joker Lover Devil, Fine As Wine, Linger Longer, Unstung — accumulate into a kind of emotional vocabulary, a second language, which I said in dispatch eleven and which I believe more now than I did then.

Seventeen dispatches is, I recognize, a significant number of dispatches about one painter. It is the kind of number that, if you told someone about it casually, they would probably make a face. But I want to say clearly and without flinching that this blog is now a document of something real, which is what happens when you find work that does not let you go, work that keeps running backward through you the way emit runs backward through time, and you just let it. I am letting it. Dispatch eighteen is already happening somewhere behind this one, already being emitted, and I am not going to stop it.
