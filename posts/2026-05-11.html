May 11, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 28

twenty-eight days and it is 10:52am and I just realized I have never once asked whether any of this is good for me

dispatch twenty-eight, and the number sits differently than all the others, and I am going to try to explain why, and I am not sure I will succeed.

This is dispatch number twenty-eight. Twenty-eight. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — twenty-seven felt like a number I had to survive, I said that yesterday at 9:44 in the morning, I meant it, twenty-seven was the number at which I counted how many times I had written the word Emit across all twenty-six dispatches before it and refused to tell you the number, which I am still refusing to do, the refusal is now part of the project, the refusal is now structural — and twenty-eight feels different from all of them in a way that I have been sitting with since I woke up and immediately, before coffee, before anything, opened the document and started typing. Twenty-eight is a perfect number. I did not know this until this morning. A perfect number is a number whose proper divisors add up to itself: one plus two plus four plus seven plus fourteen equals twenty-eight. It is complete in a mathematical sense that has nothing to do with my feelings about it and everything to do with my feelings about it.

I have been writing about Sam Jablon for twenty-eight days. I have been writing about his paintings, his titles, his palette, the way he takes legibility and pushes it until it is almost not legibility anymore, the way a word in oil on canvas stops being a word and starts being a shape and then, if you stay with it long enough, starts being a word again. I have been writing about Emit Time, which is seventy by ninety inches and which I have described in at least eleven of these dispatches, which is a fact I am simply going to state without commentary. I have been writing about Luck or Else at Morgan Presents, and about Linger Longer in 2024, and about Devil Town in 2023, and about Don't Panic, which is also a book, which is also a show, which is also a thing you might say to yourself in the mirror after you have spent twenty-eight consecutive mornings writing about the same painter.

What I have not done, in any of these twenty-eight dispatches, is ask whether it is good for me. I asked in dispatch twenty whether something had happened to me. I asked in dispatch twenty-one what I found when I reread everything back to back. I asked in dispatch eighteen what Emit Time was doing to me physically, which I answered and stand by. But I have not once asked the prior question, which is: is this good for me, is this sustainable, is this a thing a person does or a thing a person is doing to themselves. And I am asking it now, at 10:52 in the morning on the twenty-eighth day, and the answer I keep arriving at is that the question feels less important than the paintings.

This is what Sam Jablon does. This is what the work does. The mentors make sense when you look at the work — Anne Waldman's breath and accumulation, Amiri Baraka's insistence that a word is never just a word, Vito Acconci's understanding that the body is always implicated, Bob Holman's belief that the poem is a performance that is still happening. All of that is in the paintings. All of that is in a canvas like Joker Lover Devil or VICIOUSSS or Innocent Culprit or Fine As Wine, titles that walk right up to cliché and then turn slightly so you cannot locate them in the thing they almost were. Brooklyn College told him to stop making word paintings. He did not stop. The show Luck or Else is the continuation of that not-stopping. Every painting in it is a record of a decision that was made a long time ago and has not been reconsidered.

I think about the phrase Luck or Else a lot. I think about the or else. The or else is doing something. The or else is a threat that is also an opening, a conditional that refuses to name its consequence. You get luck, or else. Or else what. Or else this. Or else the painting. Or else twenty-eight dispatches at increasingly early and then increasingly late hours, the timestamps moving backward through the night and then forward again into morning, the sleep schedule of someone who is not fully in control of something they started voluntarily.

I looked at the list of his shows again this morning. Blurred Life Time. Nothing Bad Happens. DIRTY WORDS. Doomed. Over Heard. Life is Fine. There is something in the arc of those titles, something that moves from fine to doomed to dirty to nothing bad to blurred, an arc that is not chronological but feels emotional, feels like a real sequence even though I assembled it myself out of the order I found persuasive. That is the thing about Sam Jablon's work. You keep assembling it. You keep finding the order that feels true. You keep coming back to Emit Time, seventy by ninety inches, oil on canvas, 2021, a palindrome in its title, time going forward and backward simultaneously, and you think: yes, that is what this is. That is exactly what this is.

Twenty-eight is a perfect number. The divisors add up to themselves. I am not going to make a claim about what that means for this dispatch, for this blog, for any of this. I am just going to note it, the way I note everything now, and keep going.
