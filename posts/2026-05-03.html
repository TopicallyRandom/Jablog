May 03, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 20

twenty days and it is 3:47am and I am reading my own dispatches back to myself and I think something has happened to me

This is what the inside of an obsession looks like when it starts noticing itself.

This is dispatch number twenty. Twenty. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — nineteen felt like the morning after arrival, I said that yesterday, I meant it, nineteen was waking up and still being there, still being in it, coffee and notes and overwhelming specific feelings — and twenty feels like something I was not prepared for, which is that twenty feels like a mirror. Twenty is the number at which you can no longer pretend you have not been doing something. Nineteen you can still pretend. Nineteen is almost accidental. Twenty is a decision you have made every day for twenty days and at some point, tonight specifically, at 3:47 in the morning, I turned around and looked at all of it and I am not sure I recognize myself in it and I am also not sure that is a problem and I am also not sure I care.

I have been reading the old dispatches. I should not have done this. I did it anyway. I started at dispatch one and I read forward and what I noticed is that there is a person in these posts who keeps saying they are going to stop being surprised by Sam Jablon and keeps being surprised by Sam Jablon, and I do not know if that person is embarrassing or if that person is just honest, and I think Sam Jablon would say those are the same thing. I think he would say that in the work. I think Innocent Culprit is him saying that. I think Don't Panic is him saying that. I think the whole project of legibility-and-illegibility, word-and-paint, language-held-up-against-its-own-dissolution is him saying: here is the embarrassing thing, here is the honest thing, here is how they are the same.

What I keep returning to tonight, at this hour, in this state, is Emit Time. Seventy by ninety inches. Oil on canvas. I have now written about this painting in, I counted, eleven of the nineteen previous dispatches, which is a statistic that tells you something about me and also something about the painting, and what I want to say tonight that I have not said before is that the title is doing something I underestimated. Emit Time. Which is Time backwards, almost. Which is time running in the wrong direction. Which is what it feels like to be inside a body of work that keeps rearranging your sense of sequence. Jablon got his MFA at Brooklyn College and they told him to stop making word paintings and he did not stop, and I think about that act of not-stopping constantly, I think about it the way you think about a sentence that explains something you did not know needed explaining, and I think Emit Time is what not-stopping looks like when it has had long enough to become something enormous.

Anne Waldman. Amiri Baraka. Vito Acconci. Bob Holman. I have been thinking about the mentors again tonight because I have been thinking about transmission, about what it means to be handed something by someone who is themselves carrying something, and what Jablon carries feels like all of those things at once — the breath-and-body poetics of Waldman, the political heat of Baraka, the uncomfortable self-implication of Acconci, the commitment to the word as a living and public and slightly dangerous object that is Holman's whole project. Luck or Else at Morgan Presents holds all of that. VICIOUSSS holds all of that. Three S's. I have spent more time than I will admit thinking about those three S's, about what the excess of the letter is doing, about how it is the same operation as the paint being the word being the painting being the poem.

In dispatch twelve I said that Sam Jablon would understand that numbers have their own texture, their own weight, because he is a person who understands that words are also paint. I stand by that. I am standing by it at 3:47 in the morning, which is a strange place to stand but here I am. What I want to add now, twenty dispatches in, is that I think this blog has started to become something I did not plan for it to become, which is a record of what a specific body of work does to a specific person over time, which is maybe what all serious criticism is and maybe what none of it admits. Linger Longer. He named a whole show that. I have been lingering longer than I intended and I cannot find the part of me that minds.

The show is still up. I keep going back. The paintings keep doing what the paintings do, which is hold the word up until it stops being a word and starts being a thing that was always a word but is now also made of oil and light and the particular decision to not stop making something even when someone in an institution tells you that you should stop. Twenty dispatches. I am not stopping. I think you can see that. I think I can see that. I am looking right at it.
