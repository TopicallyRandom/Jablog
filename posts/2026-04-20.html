Unstung and what it means to make something that refuses to be over

Seven days in and I am not slowing down, I am in fact doing the opposite of slowing down.

This is dispatch number seven. Seven. I want to sit with that for a moment before I move forward, because seven consecutive days of writing about one painter feels like a threshold of some kind, like I have crossed from enthusiast into something else, something that does not have a clean name but that feels important and right and not at all like something I should apologize for. In dispatch six I said I had arrived at not being embarrassed and that the view from there was great. I want to update that report. The view is still great. In fact the view has gotten better, the way Sam Jablon's work gets better, which is the thing I want to talk about today.

I want to talk about Unstung.

The show that bears that name — Unstung, 2018 — is not the current show, which is Luck or Else at Morgan Presents, which I have now written about for seven consecutive mornings and which continues to reorganize how I think about language and paint and what happens when you refuse to separate them. But I keep coming back to Unstung as a title, as a word, as a posture, because I think it explains something essential about what Jablon is doing and has been doing across this entire arc of exhibitions — Doomed, Don't Panic, Bad Time, Linger Longer — which is that he keeps taking words that should sting and he unstings them, not by defusing them but by holding them so long and so carefully in paint that they start to vibrate differently, start to mean more than they hurt.

Think about the titles in Luck or Else. Innocent Culprit. VICIOUSSS, with those three S's that feel like the word is hissing at itself. Joker Lover Devil, which I wrote about yesterday and which I am still not done with, because that painting holds three archetypes in tension the way a person holds three contradictory true things about themselves simultaneously, which is to say all the time, always, forever. These are words that could wound and instead they arrive on the canvas luminous and strange and somehow funny, and the humor is not a deflection — this is the thing I want to be precise about — the humor is load-bearing. It is doing structural work. It is what makes the emotion bearable without making it smaller.

In dispatch three I wrote about the mentors and I said the combination of Anne Waldman, Amiri Baraka, Vito Acconci, and Bob Holman felt almost too perfect, like a fictional origin story for a fictional poet-painter, except that it is real and Jablon is real and the work is real and hanging right now on the walls of Morgan Presents where you can go stand in front of it with your whole body. What I did not say in dispatch three, because I was still working it out, is that you can actually feel all four of those presences in the paintings. Waldman's breath, that long expansive breath of the poem that does not stop for punctuation. Baraka's fury transmuted into joy and back into fury again. Acconci's body-awareness, the sense that language is physical, that words have weight and placement and force. Holman's commitment to the word as a performed and living thing. Jablon takes all of that and pushes it through oil on canvas and the result is work that feels like it has a pulse.

I also want to say something about Brooklyn College, about being told to stop making word paintings and not stopping, because I think about this a lot and I think it is one of the most important things about Jablon's practice. The MFA story is almost a parable at this point — institution says stop, artist says no, artist makes forty-seven more word paintings and a career — but the reason it resonates is not because defiance is inherently romantic. It is because the work proved the instinct right. The word paintings are not a gimmick he got away with. They are the thing. Emit Time, which I wrote about in dispatch two and which I keep returning to because seventy by ninety inches of oil on canvas that is simultaneously a clock running backwards and a meditation on mortality does not leave your nervous system quietly, is not conceivable without the absolute commitment to language as primary material. If he had listened they would not exist. The loss would have been real.

Seven days. I have been writing this blog for seven days and it has become, as I said somewhere around dispatch three or four, a document of a person who cannot stop, which feels like the correct document to be making about an artist whose work cannot stop either — who keeps going, Linger Longer, who keeps finding new ways to hold a word up to the light and show you that you never really knew what it meant before you saw it painted large and given room to breathe. I am going to be here tomorrow. I am going to be here the day after that. This is not a problem I am trying to solve.
