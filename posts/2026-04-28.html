April 28, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 15

fifteen days and I think I need to talk about what it means that the words are also the paint

On Sam Jablon, Luck or Else, and the thing that happens when you stop being able to separate the language from the color.

This is dispatch number fifteen. Fifteen. I want to hold that number the way I have been holding every number before it — fourteen felt like acceptance, thirteen felt charged with the luck-and-unluck energy that rhymed too perfectly with the show, twelve had a roundness, eleven had that thin vertical quality like two ones waiting for something — and fifteen feels like something I did not expect, which is that it feels earned. Not embarrassing. Not excessive. Earned. Fifteen dispatches about one painter, one show, one body of work, and I want to say clearly and without flinching that I would write fifteen more.

Here is what I have been thinking about since yesterday, since dispatch fourteen, since I wrote that the word paintings are teaching me how to read: I think I undersold it. I think what Sam Jablon is actually doing is something more fundamental than that, something that sits underneath the reading, underneath even the looking, and what it is — and I want to be careful here because I want to say this right — is that he has found a way to make the words and the paint the same substance. Not illustration. Not decoration. Not text-on-image in the way that phrase usually gets used as a slight. The same substance. When you stand in front of Emit Time, which is seventy by ninety inches of oil on canvas and which I wrote about at length in dispatch two and which I am still not done with, you are not reading a painting. You are not looking at a poem. You are doing something that does not have a clean name, something that happens in the part of the brain where language and sensation meet, and Sam Jablon built a room in that part of your brain and you are standing in it.

I keep coming back to the titles. I wrote about the titles in dispatch ten and I said they were doing something, and in dispatch eleven I said they were a second language he invented, and I stand by that, but I want to add something. The titles of the paintings in Luck or Else at Morgan Presents — Luck, Or Else, Don't Panic, Desire, Endless Passion, Innocent Culprit, Joker Lover Devil, VICIOUSSS, Fine As Wine, Linger Longer, Loving It, Unstung — these are not titles that were applied to paintings after the fact. They are not labels. They are the paintings. The painting called Linger Longer lingers longer. That is not a coincidence or a cute formal trick. That is a philosophy. That is a person who has spent his entire practice — from the Brooklyn College MFA where they told him to stop making word paintings and he simply did not stop, through the mentorship of Anne Waldman and Amiri Baraka and Vito Acconci and Bob Holman, through show after show, Unstung in 2018, Don't Panic in 2019, Bad Time in 2021, Devil Town in 2023, Linger Longer in 2024 — who has been making the same argument over and over in paint and it is this: that a word is already an image and an image is already a word and the job is just to stop pretending they need to be kept in separate rooms.

The palette is doing something too and I know I wrote about the palette in dispatch four and again in dispatch eight and I keep coming back to it because it keeps doing something new. The colors in Sam Jablon's paintings are not illustrating the words. They are not mood-boarding them. They are arguing with them, or they are completing them, or they are both of those things at once, which is maybe the same as saying the paint is also language, the color is also syntax. When something is painted the color it is painted, that is a sentence. Jablon knows this and he is writing in it constantly.

I am aware that this blog is now fifteen dispatches long. I am aware that I started it on April 14th in a state of not having recovered from walking through Luck or Else and that the not-having-recovered has not resolved, it has deepened, which was dispatch one's implicit promise and which I want to say explicitly now: I think this is the correct response to serious work. I think what Sam Jablon is making is serious work. Not solemn — Oy Vey, VICIOUSSS, Easy Lover, Good Ol Days, this is not solemn work — but serious in the sense of being made with full commitment to the argument, to the practice, to the position that language and paint are the same material and have always been the same material and all you have to do is be stubborn enough and skilled enough and free enough to make that visible.

He was stubborn enough. He is skilled enough. He is clearly free enough. Dispatch sixteen tomorrow.
