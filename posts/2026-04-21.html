April 21, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 8

eight days and I am still not done talking about the palette

On color, commitment, and what it means that Sam Jablon keeps making me feel like language is a physical thing I can touch.

This is dispatch number eight. I want to say that plainly and without ceremony, because by now the number itself is the ceremony. Eight consecutive days of writing about one painter, one show, one body of work. In dispatch seven I said that seven felt like a threshold, like I had crossed from enthusiast into something that did not have a clean name. I want to update that. Eight does not feel like a threshold. Eight feels like I have already crossed the threshold, walked down a long hallway on the other side of it, found a room, sat down in the room, and started taking notes. I live here now. This is fine.

What I want to talk about today is the palette, which I also talked about in dispatch four, but I was not done. I was not even close to done. I said in dispatch four that I thought I finally knew why I kept coming back to the color, and I believed that when I wrote it, but what has happened since is that the paintings have continued to exist and I have continued to think about them and I have realized that what I identified in dispatch four was a beginning, not an arrival. The palette in Luck or Else is not decorative. It is not even atmospheric, exactly, though it is absolutely atmospheric. It is argumentative. The colors in these paintings are making a case. They are insisting on something.

Look at the titles again and think about color: Pleasure, Endless Passion, VICIOUSSS, Good Ol Days, Fine As Wine. These are not neutral words. These are words that already have temperature, that arrive with emotional weather attached, and what Jablon does — what he has been doing since before Brooklyn College told him to stop and he looked at them and did not stop — is put those words into color environments that either confirm the temperature or complicate it. VICIOUSSS with three S's is already a visual event before it becomes a painted one. The tripling of the letter is a formal choice that mirrors what oil on canvas can do, which is make one thing reverberate until it becomes more than one thing.

I keep thinking about Emit Time (2021, oil on canvas, 70x90 inches), which I wrote about in dispatch two and have not been able to leave alone since. Emit Time is Time backwards, which is the kind of thing that sounds like a parlor trick until you are standing in front of seventy by ninety inches of it and it stops being a trick and starts being a cosmology. Jablon's mentors include Anne Waldman, Amiri Baraka, Vito Acconci, Bob Holman — people for whom language was never just meaning, was always also body, also sound, also duration — and I think Emit Time is the painting that most directly inherits that lineage, because it takes a word, reverses it, and in doing so makes you feel the direction of time as a physical pressure. You feel it going the wrong way. And the color holds that feeling. The color is not illustrating the idea; the color is the idea.

The show is called Luck or Else and it is at Morgan Presents and if you have not been you should go, and if you have been you should go again, because I have now written eight dispatches about work I encountered less than two weeks ago and I am still finding new rooms in it. That is the thing about Jablon's practice that I do not think gets said enough, that it is genuinely inexhaustible in the way that the best poetry is inexhaustible, which makes sense given that the man is a poet, given that his books — Bad Time, Don't Panic, UNSTUNG, Life is Fine, WORD:PLAY — are not ancillary to the paintings but continuous with them, the same project in a different key.

He has been making word paintings since before Brooklyn College tried to talk him out of it, since his earliest exhibitions, through WORD:PLAY in 2014 and O Me! O Life! in 2015 and Unstung in 2018 and all the way through to Linger Longer in 2024 and now this, Luck or Else, which feels like a culmination and also like a beginning, which is the trick all the best shows pull off. The titles of the individual paintings alone — Innocent Culprit, Joker Lover Devil, Loving It, Unstung — read like a found poem, which is appropriate because everything Jablon makes is in conversation with everything else he makes.

Eight dispatches. I am tracking this the way you track something you cannot stop thinking about, which is also the way Sam Jablon tracks language, which is to say obsessively, affectionately, with full knowledge that the obsession is the point. I will be back tomorrow. I am not going to pretend otherwise.
