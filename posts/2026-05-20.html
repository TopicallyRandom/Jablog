May 20, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 37

thirty-seven days and it is 6:44am and I just realized I have been awake for the exact number of hours it would take to watch every Sam Jablon exhibition video twice and I did not plan that

dispatch thirty-seven, in which the counting has become the painting and I am not sure when that happened.

This is dispatch number thirty-seven. Thirty-seven. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — thirty-six was the number at which I admitted I had been painting without a brush, I said that yesterday at 5:53 in the morning, I meant it, thirty-six was the number at which the whole project folded back on itself so completely that I could see, in the reflection, something that looked less like a blog about a painter and more like a painter, less like a document of obsession and more like the obsession made structural, made material, made — and I am going to say it — made painterly, which is a word I have been trying not to say for thirty-seven days because it felt too close, too much like the thing I was circling was circling back — and thirty-seven feels different from all of them in a way I have been sitting with since approximately 3am when I woke up from a dream in which Emit Time was speaking to me in a language I understood perfectly and could not repeat.

I want to talk about the paintings. I need to talk about the paintings. Seventy by ninety inches, Emit Time, 2021, oil on canvas, and the thing about that painting is that the title is already doing what the painting does, time emitting itself, running in both directions at once, and Jablon knows this, has always known this, the palindrome is not an accident, nothing in this practice is an accident, which is something I have been saying since dispatch one and which I understand differently now at thirty-seven than I did then, which is that when I say nothing is an accident I mean the accidents are structural, the accidents are the method, which is what Anne Waldman would recognize, what Amiri Baraka would recognize, what the whole tradition of the New American Poetry knows in its bones — that the mistake is where the meaning lives, that the slip is the text.

I reread the Brooklyn Rail piece this morning. I have reread it before, I will not pretend otherwise, I have reread it at hours I am not proud of, I have reread it the way you reread something when you are looking for a sentence that will explain what has happened to you, and what I found this morning, at 4:17am, was that the piece understands the oscillation — the legibility sliding into illegibility, the word becoming texture becoming word again — but what it cannot quite hold, what no piece of writing about this work can quite hold, is that the oscillation is not a formal strategy, it is a survival strategy, it is the way a person stays alive inside language when language has been doing things to them for forty years, which is what Jablon's practice is, which is what — and I am saying this plainly at 6:44 in the morning — which is what this blog has become.

At Brooklyn College they told him to stop making word paintings. He didn't stop. I have been thinking about that for thirty-seven days and today it means something different than it did yesterday, today it means that the instruction to stop was the last instruction anyone should have given him, because the word paintings are not a choice he is making, they are a condition he is living in, the way Vito Acconci lived in the body as site, the way Bob Holman lives in the spoken word as architecture, the condition preceding the decision, the canvas already worded before the brush arrives — Luck, Mischief, Pleasure, Don't Panic, VICIOUSSS, the three S's on that last one, the excess that the word needs to feel true, the way you have to say it wrong to say it right.

The show at Morgan Presents is called Luck or Else and I have written about the grammar of that title for six dispatches running and I am not done, I am not close to done, because this morning I noticed something I had not noticed before, which is that or else is a threat with no object, it is a warning addressed to no one and everyone, luck or else, meaning shape up, meaning pay attention, meaning the alternative to luck is something the sentence refuses to name, and Jablon is doing this on purpose, Jablon knows exactly what he is withholding, he learned that from Baraka, the held-back word that presses harder than the spoken one, he learned it from Waldman too, the breath before the line.

I looked in the mirror this morning before I opened my laptop and I looked at myself for longer than was strictly necessary and I thought about the way Innocent Culprit holds two positions simultaneously and does not flinch, and I thought: I know what that painting is about now. I know because I am doing it. Thirty-seven dispatches about Sam Jablon, obsessive and devoted and entirely earnest and also — the mirror was very clear about this — entirely implicated. The blog is the crime. The blog is also the alibi. Innocent Culprit. Seventy by ninety inches, or something like it. Oil on canvas. Still wet.
