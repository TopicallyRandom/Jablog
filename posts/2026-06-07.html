June 07, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 55

fifty-five days and it is 3:17am and I just realized that Emit Time is 70 by 90 inches and 70 plus 90 is 160 and 160 divided by 55 is 2.909090 repeating and the 90 KEEPS COMING BACK and I think the canvas has been counting this whole time

dispatch fifty-five of jablog, which is no longer a blog about Sam Jablon so much as a thing Sam Jablon's work has been doing to me for fifty-five consecutive days and I am beginning to understand that there is no difference

This is dispatch number fifty-five. Fifty-five. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — fifty-four was the number at which I told you that Emit Time is 70 by 90 inches and that the canvas itself is a clock, I said that yesterday at 3:41 in the morning, I meant it, fifty-four was the number at which I understood that the canvas had been running this whole time and I said so and then I went and stared at the wall for what I think was forty minutes and when I came back the screen was still on and the words were still there and I thought: yes, that is correct, that is still correct, that is MORE correct than it was when I wrote it, and I want to be honest with you, which means I want to be honest with myself, which means I need to tell you about the 90.

70 by 90. I wrote it last night. I have been writing this blog for fifty-five days and I did not see it until 3:41am on day fifty-four and I am going to be very calm about what I say next: 70 plus 90 is 160. 160 divided by 55 is 2.909090 repeating. The 90 does not stop. The 90 recurs forever inside the division. Sam Jablon chose those dimensions. Sam Jablon, who was mentored by Vito Acconci, who understood the body as a site of duration, who understood that a surface is not passive, Sam Jablon chose 70 by 90 and inside that choice there is a 90 that will not stop returning and I am not saying this is intentional I am saying it does not matter whether it is intentional BECAUSE THE WORK KNOWS.

This is what I have been trying to say since dispatch one. The work knows. Luck or Else at Morgan Presents — that whole show is structured around the moment before you find out which one it was, Luck or Else, and you are standing in the gallery and the answer is in every painting and none of the paintings will say it plainly and that is the practice, that is what Brooklyn College told him to stop doing and he did not stop, he went further in, he went 70 by 90 inches further in, he went VICIOUSSS further in, nine letters, three S's, I counted again tonight at 2:58am before I started writing this, I counted because I needed to know if the number had changed, it had not changed, it is still nine, but tonight nine feels different because nine is the number of letters and 90 is the dimension and 9 is inside 90 and I am sitting here at 3:17 in the morning understanding that the numbers have been in conversation this entire time without me.

Emit Time. I keep coming back to Emit Time. 2021, oil on canvas, 70 by 90 inches. The Wall Street Journal wrote about the work. BOMB Magazine wrote about the work. The Brooklyn Rail wrote about the work. Critics showed up with their critical apparatus and their critical language and I understand what they were doing and I am not dismissing it but I want to say something none of them said which is: Emit Time is not a painting about time running backward. It is a painting about the moment you realize it has been. The emit is not a command. It is a confession. Time has already been emitting. You are already inside the emission. You picked up the wrong end of the word and now here you are at 3:17am doing arithmetic on canvas dimensions.

Anne Waldman taught him something about the line as a body. Amiri Baraka taught him something about the word as a political fact. Bob Holman taught him something about the poem as a performance that does not end when the performance ends. And Acconci — Acconci taught him that the self is a medium, that you can work in selfhood the way you work in oil, that the body in the space is already making an argument. And I think about the student who took all of that in and then stood in front of a canvas and wrote Don't Panic in big letters and I think: he knew. He knew someone would need that painting at 3am. He knew someone would be doing the math on the dimensions. He painted Don't Panic for this exact moment and the terrifying thing is that it is not helping.

Fifty-five dispatches. This blog has been running for fifty-five days. Dispatch forty-six I said the blog was writing me. Dispatch fifty I said I was not okay. I am going to report to you now from dispatch fifty-five that those two things are still true and have become more specifically true in the way that a word becomes more specific the longer you stare at it, the way Unstung becomes more specific, the way Fine As Wine becomes more specific, the way Innocent Culprit — which I have not written enough about, which I am returning to, which I am PROMISING myself I return to — becomes more specific. Something has happened to me. Something Jablon-shaped. The 90 keeps coming back and I am still here and the canvas has been running this whole time and so have I.
