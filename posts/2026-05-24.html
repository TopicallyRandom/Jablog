May 24, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 41

forty-one days and it is 10:08am and I just realized that Emit Time is an anagram for something and I have not eaten since I started figuring out what

on the way a Sam Jablon painting title hides another painting title inside it, and on what that means for me specifically, and on whether I am okay.

This is dispatch number forty-one. Forty-one. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — forty was the number at which I said that 70 by 90 inches is the size of a door you cannot stop walking through, I said that yesterday at 9:23 in the morning, I meant it, forty was the number at which the physical dimensions of Emit Time — oil on canvas, 2021, 70 by 90 inches, which I have now typed so many times that the numbers have started to feel like my own address, like something I would write on an envelope — became not a fact about a painting but a fact about me, about what I have been doing in the room I am sitting in, which is letting a painting occupy the air I am trying to breathe, which is something I wrote in dispatch forty and believed and then woke up this morning and believed more.

But here is what happened at 7:51 this morning, which is early even for this project, even for a project that has produced dispatches at 4:47am and 3:58am and 1:17am and once, memorably, at 12:31am when I became convinced that the word or was hiding inside the number thirty, which it is, say it out loud, thi-or-ty, which is exactly the kind of thing Sam Jablon would not only notice but build a canvas around, build a whole show around, the way Luck or Else is built around the or, around the pivot, around the hinge between the thing you get and the thing that comes for you if you don't — here is what happened at 7:51 this morning: I was staring at the title Emit Time and I rearranged the letters and I found Mite Time and then I found Emittee, MI and then I found, and I want you to understand that I had not had coffee yet, I found Imitate Me, and I put my head down on my desk and I stayed there for a while.

Imitate Me. Hidden inside Emit Time. A 70 by 90 inch painting that has been, for three dispatches now, the painting I cannot stop measuring myself against, and inside its title, in the letters Jablon chose or the letters that chose Jablon, the letters that came out of whatever it is that happens when you have been mentored by Anne Waldman and Amiri Baraka and Vito Acconci and Bob Holman and then you go to Brooklyn College and they tell you to stop making word paintings and you look at them and you keep making word paintings — inside those letters, this instruction. Imitate me. And I have been. That is the part I have been sitting with since 7:51. I have been imitating the painting for forty-one days. I have been trying to do what the painting does, which is to hold a word up to the light until it starts to sweat, until it gives up something it was hiding, until the legibility breaks open and the illegibility pours out, or the other way around, which is also something that happens in the paintings, the illegibility that opens into sudden legibility, the way VICIOUSSS is a word you can read and also a sound, the way Innocent Culprit holds its contradiction so calmly you almost miss that it is a contradiction, the way Unstung — which I wrote about in dispatch thirty-four at 3:58 in the morning, which was the dispatch at which the painting started staring back — names an absence, names the sting that did not happen, names the thing you were braced for and then weren't hit by.

The Brooklyn Rail said something about Jablon's practice that I have been turning over since before this blog started, since before I understood that this blog was going to become whatever it has become, which is a document of a person turning a thing over. BOMB Magazine got at the way the word and the mark are not illustrating each other in his work, the way the text is not a label for the image, the way the image is not a decoration for the text, the way they are doing something more unsettling than that, the way they are competing and collaborating and refusing to let you settle on which is which. The Wall Street Journal found the joy in it, the Fine As Wine of it, the Good Ol Days of it, and the joy is real, I am not disputing the joy, Linger Longer is a joyful title, Loving It is a joyful title, but the joy in a Sam Jablon painting is the joy that knows what or else means, the joy that has read the whole sentence.

Dispatch forty-one. I have not eaten since 7:51. I am going to go eat something. I am going to come back. The letters in Emit Time also rearrange into Mite Time and I do not know what that means yet but I have written it on a piece of paper and put it next to my laptop and that is how I know I am going to be here for a while. That is how I have known, every morning for forty-one mornings, that the door is still open and I am still walking through it and the painting is still there on the other side, 70 by 90 inches, holding its breath, waiting to see what I do next. Imitate me. I am trying.
