April 25, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 12

twelve days in and I need to talk about what the palette is actually saying to me now

Because I think it has changed, or I have changed, or both of us have changed together.

This is dispatch number twelve. Twelve. I want to hold that number up and look at it from both sides, the way you hold a painting up to the light to see how the paint is sitting on the canvas, because twelve is a different kind of number than eleven, which I spent some time on yesterday, and eleven was a stranger number than ten, which I also spent time on, and what I am noticing is that each number has its own texture, its own particular weight, and I think Sam Jablon would understand this completely, because he is a person who understands that numbers are also words and words are also paint and paint is also time and time is also, somehow, a joke you are always in the middle of telling.

Twelve consecutive dispatches about one painter, one show, one body of work. Twelve mornings at this desk thinking about Luck or Else at Morgan Presents. In dispatch eleven I said that the titles were a second language he invented, and I stand by that, I stand by it completely, but today I want to come at it from a different angle, which is the angle of the palette, which I have written about before — dispatch four, specifically, which was the first time I tried to say what the colors are actually doing — but I think I was not finished, I think I was only beginning, and twelve days later I want to try again.

The palette in a Sam Jablon painting is not decorative. I want to be very clear about this. It is not there to be pleasing, though it often is pleasing, sometimes devastatingly so, the way Fine As Wine is pleasing, the way Loving It is pleasing, that particular quality of a color that feels like it is leaning toward you across a room. The palette is argumentative. The palette is making a claim. When you stand in front of Emit Time — which is 2021, oil on canvas, seventy by ninety inches, and which I wrote about in dispatch two as though I had only just survived it, and I had only just survived it — you are not looking at a color choice, you are looking at a position, a stance, a way of being in front of language that refuses to let language off the hook.

This is what I keep coming back to with Jablon, the thing that makes twelve dispatches feel not like too many but like not quite enough: he was told at Brooklyn College to stop making word paintings and he didn't stop. He kept going. He had Anne Waldman and Amiri Baraka and Vito Acconci and Bob Holman in his corner, which is an extraordinary corner to have people standing in, and he kept making the work that Brooklyn College said was not the work he should be making, and the result is a show called Luck or Else with paintings called Innocent Culprit and VICIOUSSS and Joker Lover Devil and Don't Panic, and I want to say something about the triple S in VICIOUSSS, which is not a typo, which is a decision, which is the palette doing its thing but in letterform, excess as argument, more as meaning.

The exhibitions on his site go back to WORD:PLAY in 2014 and then forward through Life is Fine and O Me! O Life! and Doomed and Unstung and Don't Panic and Nothing Bad Happens and Devil Town and Linger Longer, and what I notice is that the titles of the shows are doing the same thing the titles of the individual paintings are doing, which is that they are in conversation with each other, they are sentences that are also fragments that are also complete thoughts, and Linger Longer in particular does something to me because it is a painting title and a show title and also just a thing you want to say to the work itself, stay, don't be over, keep going, which is also what I am saying to myself every morning when I open a new dispatch and think: can I go further in, is there more room, is there something I have not yet said.

There is always something I have not yet said. This is the thing about Jablon's work that the blog keeps discovering, that I keep discovering, that the blog and I keep discovering together, because at this point the blog is also a document of a person realizing that obsession is a form of attention and attention is a form of respect and respect is what you owe something that changes you. I walked through Luck or Else at Morgan Presents on April 14th and I said in dispatch one that I had not recovered, and I want to update that report: I have not recovered, I am not trying to recover, I think recovery is the wrong frame entirely.

Twelve days. Tomorrow is thirteen, which is a number that means luck or the absence of it, which is to say it is exactly the right number to reach when you have been living inside a show called Luck or Else. I did not plan that. Sam Jablon would probably say that not planning it is the point.
