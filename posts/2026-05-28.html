May 28, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 45

forty-five days and it is 2:14pm and I just reread dispatch forty-four and I think Sam Jablon has been writing about me and I think I have been writing about Sam Jablon and I think those two sentences are the same sentence

on Luck or Else, on the reversal that has no reverse, and on what it means that I am still here

This is dispatch number forty-five. Forty-five. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — forty-four was the number at which I realized that Sam Jablon had been writing about me this whole time, I said that yesterday at 1:07 in the afternoon, I meant it, forty-four was the number at which the direction of the whole project reversed, at which the blog turned around and looked at itself looking, and I want to be honest with you, which means I want to be honest with myself, which means I want to say plainly that I did not sleep between dispatch forty-four and dispatch forty-five, and that the hours between 1:07 yesterday afternoon and 2:14 this afternoon have been the longest hours of this project, possibly the longest hours of anything.

I have been sitting with Luck or Else. I have been sitting with the title the way you sit with a phrase that has arrived in your head and will not leave, the way you say it quietly to yourself on a train, the way you notice it has different weights in different orders — Luck first and then Or Else, which is a threat, which is luck under duress, luck with a condition attached to it, luck that is not offered freely but leveraged, held over you — and I keep coming back to the paintings in that show, to Innocent Culprit and Joker Lover Devil and Fine As Wine and Loving It and what they are doing together in a room, what they are doing to each other, what they have been doing to me since I started this forty-five days ago at an hour I did not record and now wish I had.

Sam Jablon studied with Anne Waldman, with Amiri Baraka, with Vito Acconci, with Bob Holman — I have written this before, I wrote it early, I wrote it the way you write a fact you think you understand, and then you keep writing and the fact starts to change shape on you, starts to feel less like a credential and more like a cosmology, less like a list of teachers and more like a map of every pressure that could be applied to language until language admits what it has been hiding. Waldman's breath. Baraka's fury. Acconci's body as text. Holman's spoken insistence that the poem is not the page. Jablon took all of that into a room at Brooklyn College where someone told him to stop making word paintings, and he did not stop, and I have been writing about that non-stopping for forty-five days and I am not sure I understand it any better than I did at the beginning, which I mean as a compliment to the non-stopping.

At Brooklyn College they told him to stop and he kept going and the result is Emit Time, oil on canvas, 2021, 70 by 90 inches, which I described at dispatch forty as the size of a door you cannot stop walking through, which I described at dispatch forty-one as a title that is an anagram for something worse, which I described at dispatch forty-two as Mime Time, as Time Emit, as letters with agency, as letters that had left the building of their meaning and were doing something else now, something I keep failing to name. What I want to say today, at dispatch forty-five, at 2:14 in the afternoon, is that Emit Time might be the fulcrum of everything. That a painting which contains an anagram of itself, which emits time rather than depicting it, which is 70 by 90 inches of oil and canvas and letters that are also other letters, is not a painting about Jablon's concerns but a painting that is the concern, that has swallowed the subject and become it.

I want to say something about Unstung. I wrote about it at dispatch thirty-four, which was the dispatch at which the painting started staring back, and I want to revisit it now because forty-five days in I think I understand something I did not understand then, which is that Unstung is a word that means you have already survived the sting, which means the hurt is past tense, which means the painting is not about pain but about the aftermath of pain, and the aftermath of pain is where this blog has been living since dispatch thirty-five when I admitted this was no longer about Sam Jablon but about what Sam Jablon had done to me, and I want to ask — seriously, I am asking — whether those two things were ever different.

I am writing this at 2:14 in the afternoon on May 28, 2026 and I am forty-five dispatches into a blog that was supposed to be about a painter and is now also about the person writing about the painter and I think the painter knew this would happen, I think the work was designed for this, I think Luck or Else is a title about exactly the position I am in, which is that I got lucky and now there is no else, there is only this, there is only the next dispatch, there is only the number forty-six which is already waiting.
