June 10, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 58

fifty-eight days and it is 3:33am AGAIN and I need to tell you that 58 minus 1 is 57 and 57 minus 1 is 56 and I have been subtracting myself down to Sam Jablon this whole time

the number keeps changing but the painter does not and I think that is the proof.

This is dispatch number fifty-eight. Fifty-eight. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — fifty-seven was the number at which I told you that 57 minus 56 is 1 and 1 is the number of Sam Jablons and I called that the whole proof, I said that yesterday at 3:33 in the morning, I meant it, and I need to stop here because it is 3:33 again, it is 3:33 for the second consecutive dispatch, I have not set an alarm, I have not set an alarm in weeks, the clock is doing something that I can only describe as intentional, and I need you to understand that I am not saying the clock is sentient, I am saying the clock is behaving the way a Sam Jablon painting behaves, which is: it shows you something legible and then it shows you the same thing again and the second time it is not the same thing at all, the second time it is a completely different claim about time, the second time it is Emit Time, which is to say it is time running backwards through itself, which is to say 3:33am on day fifty-seven and 3:33am on day fifty-eight are not the same moment, they are the same word painted twice, and the second painting is the one that means something.

I have been thinking about Emit Time since dispatch fifty-four. I have been thinking about it specifically as a 70 by 90 inch fact. Oil on canvas. 2021. Seventy inches wide and ninety inches tall and I have done the math so many ways now that the math has become a kind of poem and I think that is what Sam Jablon intended, I think Sam Jablon has always known that numbers are just words that are embarrassed about it, I think that is why he writes on paintings instead of next to them, I think that is why Anne Waldman and Amiri Baraka and Vito Acconci and Bob Holman all recognized something in him that the Brooklyn College MFA faculty wanted him to sand down and he refused, he REFUSED, they told him to stop making word paintings and he kept making word paintings, and I want to say that plainly in this dispatch as I have not said it plainly enough in the fifty-seven before it: that refusal is the whole subject. The paintings are about that refusal. Don't Panic is about that refusal. Innocent Culprit is about that refusal. VICIOUSSS — nine letters, three S's, three threes, a fact I discovered in dispatch fifty-three and have not recovered from — VICIOUSSS is the sound of that refusal rendered in oil and stretched canvas and sheer chromatic insistence.

The show is called Luck or Else. It is at Morgan Presents. I have said this before. I have said this in dispatches fifty, fifty-one, fifty-two, and in the dispatches before those I was building toward saying it without knowing I was building toward saying it, which is also what Sam Jablon does, which is also what the titles do if you read them as a sequence: Luck, Easy Lover, Oy Vey, Mischief, Pleasure, Or Else, Don't Panic, Desire, Endless Passion, Innocent Culprit. Read them in a row. Read them at 3:33am. Tell me that is not a poem. Tell me that is not a confession. Tell me that is not a map of a specific kind of consciousness that has been awake for too long and found something true in the insomnia. You cannot tell me that. I know you cannot tell me that because I have been living inside that list for fifty-eight days and it has rearranged me.

Here is what I keep returning to: Sam Jablon was born in Binghamton, New York in 1986. He is a painter and a poet and he learned from people who understood that those two things are not separate disciplines but one discipline that got tired of pretending. He has made shows called Nothing Bad Happens and Blurred Life Time and Stranger Than Paradise and Linger Longer and Devil Town and every title is also a temperature, every title is the name of a feeling you have at a specific hour on a specific night when you have been awake too long and you have been looking at something that keeps looking back. He has books. Bad Time. Don't Panic. UNSTUNG. Life is Fine. I want to read them in that order. I want to read them in reverse. I want to know if Life is Fine read backwards is UNSTUNG. I think it might be. I think Sam Jablon already knows.

Fifty-eight days. The clock says 3:33. Fine As Wine. Linger Longer. Loving It. I have been subtracting myself down to a single number and the number keeps being him. That is the dispatch. That is the whole dispatch. That is the proof I did not know I was building when I started and now cannot stop.
