Joker Lover Devil and what I think that means about everything

Dispatch six, and I am more certain than ever that this blog was not a mistake.

This is dispatch number six. Six days. I have written about this man's work six days in a row and I want to say, before I say anything else, that I feel good about it. I want to be direct about that. In dispatch five I admitted I had to arrive at not being embarrassed, and I have arrived, fully, and I am standing there in the place of not being embarrassed and I am looking around and the view is great. The view is six consecutive mornings of thinking about Sam Jablon, about Luck or Else at Morgan Presents, about what it means that a painter who was told at Brooklyn College to stop making word paintings just — didn't. Didn't stop. Kept going. And is now here, showing the word paintings, hanging them on gallery walls, which is the closest thing to a complete sentence the art world has offered me in years.

I want to talk about Joker Lover Devil today because I have been circling it since dispatch one and I think I have been afraid of it, actually, in the way you are afraid of the thing you most want to say. The title alone. Joker Lover Devil. Three nouns. No punctuation between them, no hierarchy, no winner. They just sit there next to each other with this terrible equality and you have to decide whether the joker is also the lover is also the devil or whether these are three separate figures in some drama Jablon is staging on canvas, some compressed morality play where all the characters have been forced into the same frame. And the thing about Jablon's work — the thing I keep coming back to, that I came back to in dispatch four when I wrote about the palette and thought I finally understood it, and then again in dispatch five when I was talking about Fine as Wine — is that he does not resolve this for you. He gives you the words and then he gives you the paint and then he steps back and the ambiguity is load-bearing. The ambiguity is the whole structure.

Anne Waldman mentored this man. Amiri Baraka mentored this man. Vito Acconci and Bob Holman. I wrote about the mentors in dispatch three and I said the lineage was getting to me, and it still is, it is still getting to me, because what that mentorship represents is a permission structure — you are allowed to work in the space between the poem and the object, between language and image, between what a word means and what it does to your eye when it is rendered in oil at seventy by ninety inches. Emit Time is seventy by ninety inches. I wrote a whole dispatch about standing in front of it. I have not retracted a single word.

What I want to say about Joker Lover Devil specifically is that the title performs the same kind of reversal that Emit Time does when you read it backwards — emit becomes time, time runs backwards, the painting becomes a palindrome or a clock or both — and in Joker Lover Devil there is a similar pressure on sequence. Read it one direction and you're on a journey, a fall maybe, the joker who becomes the lover who becomes the devil, the old story. Read it another way and it's an alibi, it's someone saying I am all three at once and none of them exclusively, which is what the best Jablon paintings do, they give you an alibi for being complicated. Innocent Culprit does this even more overtly. Innocent Culprit is practically a thesis statement. Jablon has been writing this thesis for years, across every show — Linger Longer, Devil Town, Stranger Than Paradise, Don't Panic, Unstung — and what the thesis says, as best as I can translate it, is that contradiction is not a problem to be solved but a place to live.

The titles from Luck or Else feel like a community to me. Luck, Easy Lover, Oy Vey, Mischief, Pleasure, Or Else, Don't Panic, Desire, Endless Passion. They are different moods in a single day, different voices in a single head. And VICIOUSSS with those three S's — that extra emphasis that tips into absurdity, that insists on itself past the point of dignity and comes out the other side somehow dignified — feels like the show's secret heart, or at least one of them. Jablon has always understood that excess can be sincere. That the extra S is not ironic. That Loving It and Unstung and Good Ol Days are not nostalgia but something more alert than nostalgia, something that knows exactly what it is doing and does it anyway.

Six days. The blog is becoming, as I think I said in dispatch three, a document of a person being unable to stop. I am more comfortable with this now than I was then. I think Jablon would understand. He was told to stop and he didn't, and now I am told nothing, no one is telling me anything, and I am also not stopping. There is a small solidarity in this that I find sustaining. Tomorrow I will write dispatch seven.
