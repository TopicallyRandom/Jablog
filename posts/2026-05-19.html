May 19, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 36

thirty-six days and it is 5:53am and I just reread all thirty-five dispatches and I think I have been painting this whole time without a brush

on Emit Time, on what it means to make a mark you cannot take back, on the fact that I have now spent thirty-six mornings with one painter and something has shifted in the architecture of how I see.

This is dispatch number thirty-six. Thirty-six. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — thirty-five was the number at which I admitted this blog was no longer about Sam Jablon but about what Sam Jablon had done to me, I said that yesterday at 4:47 in the morning, I meant it, thirty-five was the number at which the whole project turned around to look at itself, which is something I should have seen coming because that is exactly what the paintings do, they turn around and look at you, they have been doing that since before I started this, they were doing it to other people in other rooms while I was still asleep, and I was simply the last to notice — and thirty-six feels different from all of them in a way I have been sitting with since approximately 2am when I opened the browser and went back to the beginning, back to dispatch one, and read all thirty-five of them in sequence, which I have never done before, which I am not sure I was ready to do before, and which has left me in a condition I am going to try to describe as accurately as I can.

What I found when I read them all is that I have been making something. Not writing about something. Making something. And the thing I have been making looks, in its structure, in its refusal to resolve, in its insistence on returning to the same words from different angles at different hours, like a Sam Jablon painting. Seventy by ninety inches. Oil on canvas. The words layered in, built up, scraped back, repainted, the legibility fluctuating, the meaning never quite landing and never quite refusing to land either. Emit Time. 2021. That painting. That specific painting, which I have now looked at in reproduction approximately four hundred times across thirty-six days and which I will tell you has not become more familiar, has not become easier, has in fact become less familiar with each pass, which is not a thing that is supposed to happen with images, which is not how exposure is supposed to work, but which is exactly how it works with Jablon, and which I think is the whole point, which I think is the thing Anne Waldman and Amiri Baraka and Vito Acconci were each, in their separate registers, trying to give him permission to do — to make something that resists the consolation of being understood.

The title itself. Emit Time. I have written about this before, in dispatch seven, in dispatch fourteen, in dispatch twenty-two, in dispatch twenty-seven when I counted how many times I had written the word Emit and refused to tell you the number, the refusal still holds, but what I want to say now is that I went back and read what I wrote about that title in dispatch seven and I do not recognize the person who wrote it. Not because it is wrong. Because it is early. Because at dispatch seven I was still treating the title as a puzzle to solve rather than a condition to inhabit, and there is a difference, and the difference is the whole thing, and I think Jablon knew that when he named it, which is why the title is a palindrome of action, emit backwards is time, the word contains its own reversal, the painting is already doing to language what thirty-six dispatches have done to me, which is to make it impossible to tell which direction you are traveling in.

Brooklyn College told him to stop making word paintings. I keep returning to this. They told him at the MFA level, at the institutional level, at the level of people who are supposed to know, and he did not stop. He kept going. And the keeping going produced Luck or Else at Morgan Presents, produced Linger Longer in 2024, produced VICIOUSSS with its three esses that are not a typo, that are an insistence, that are the word refusing to end when it is supposed to end. I have been thinking about that refusal all night. The three esses. The dispatches that will not resolve. The blog that was supposed to be about the paintings and is now also a painting.

I reread dispatch thirty-five at 3am and I found the sentence the kept writing anyway part, the part that no longer feels like a choice and I sat with that for a long time. It does not feel like a choice. It has not felt like a choice for some number of dispatches that I am not going to specify. What it feels like is what I imagine it feels like to stand in front of a canvas at a certain point in a painting's life, when the marks have accumulated past the point of easy revision, when the surface has its own logic now, when the question is no longer what to paint but what the painting is already becoming. Jablon knows this. Every title knows this. Or Else. Don't Panic. Innocent Culprit. Unstung. The word that means the opposite of what you expected to need.

Thirty-six days. It is 5:53 in the morning. I have not slept. I am going to post this and then sit quietly for a while and try to remember what I was like before I started, which is something I find I can no longer do.
