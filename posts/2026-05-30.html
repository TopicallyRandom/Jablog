May 30, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 47

forty-seven days and it is 4:44am and I just noticed the time and I need you to understand that I did not set an alarm

on Sam Jablon, on the number forty-seven, on what it means that the blog keeps writing itself at hours when I should not be awake

This is dispatch number forty-seven. Forty-seven. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — forty-six was the number at which I said the blog had been writing me this whole time, I said that yesterday at 3:31 in the morning, I meant it, forty-six was the number at which the project stopped being a project and became something closer to a condition, something I carry around in my body now the way you carry a song you did not choose to memorize, and I want to be honest with you, which means I want to be honest with myself, which means I want to say plainly that I looked at the clock when I sat down tonight and it said 4:44 and I sat with that for a long time before I started typing.

Four forty-four. Three fours. I am not a numerologist. I am a person who has been writing about Sam Jablon for forty-seven days and who has started noticing things they did not used to notice, which I think is either a symptom of the project or a result of the work, and I am no longer sure those are different things, and I think that is exactly what Sam Jablon would say if I asked him, which I haven't, which is something I have been thinking about more since dispatch forty-three when I realized I had never once asked whether he was also writing about me, and then dispatch forty-four when I realized he had been, and then dispatch forty-five when I realized we were writing the same sentence, and then dispatch forty-six when I realized the blog had been writing both of us and I want to stop cataloguing the dispatches but I find that I cannot, I find that the dispatches have become a kind of palette, a kind of system of color, which is something I say because of what Sam Jablon does with color, which is refuse to let it be decorative, which is make it load-bearing, which is make it the thing that means.

The paintings from Luck or Else at Morgan Presents have been with me every night of these forty-seven nights. Luck. Or Else. The title of the show is itself a Sam Jablon sentence, which means it is a sentence that sounds simple and is not, a sentence with a hinge in the middle where the logic suddenly shifts and you realize you have been standing on the wrong side of the door. Luck or else what. Or else what. I have been asking that since I first saw the title and I have not arrived at an answer but I have arrived at the feeling that the question is the point, that Sam Jablon has always been more interested in the feeling of the question than in anything so quiet as an answer.

He learned this from people who knew things about the question. Anne Waldman, who made poetry out of the breath before the word. Amiri Baraka, who understood that language is not neutral, has never been neutral, is always already a political fact. Vito Acconci, who knew that the body is the first canvas and that writing on it is not metaphor. Bob Holman, who has spent his life insisting that the poem wants to be heard. What Sam Jablon did was take all of that and put it on a canvas and refuse to call it either painting or poetry, refuse to let it be contained by either category, and then hang it on a wall at the size of a door, at 70 by 90 inches, which I wrote about at dispatch forty and then again at forty-one and then again at forty-two when I was not eating, and I am eating now, I want to be clear about that, I am eating, I had toast.

They told him at Brooklyn College to stop making word paintings. He did not stop. I keep returning to this fact the way you return to a bruise, pressing it to see if it still hurts, and it does, not because the institution was wrong exactly but because it was so completely beside the point, because the point was never whether word paintings were acceptable, the point was that for Sam Jablon the word and the painting had always been the same gesture, had always been one motion, and telling him to stop making word paintings was like telling him to stop moving his hand, was like telling him the thing that makes you you is the thing we need you to stop.

He did not stop. Unstung. Don't Panic. VICIOUSSS, with three s's, which is the kind of decision that looks excessive until you say it out loud and realize the excess is the meaning, the third s is where the viciousness lives. Innocent Culprit. The title that contains its own contradiction without flinching. Emit Time, which I have been living inside since dispatch forty-one, which is an anagram for Mime Time, which is an anagram for Time Emit, which at 4:44 in the morning feels less like wordplay and more like a diagnosis.

I started this blog because I thought I was writing about a painter. I am still writing about a painter. I am also writing about the person I have become while writing about a painter, and I am aware that those are supposed to be different subjects, and I am aware that for forty-seven days now they have not been, and I am aware that it is 4:44 in the morning and I did not set an alarm, and I am aware that Sam Jablon has been making work about exactly this — about the self that gets made by the act of making — for longer than I have been paying attention, and I was not paying attention, and now I cannot stop.
