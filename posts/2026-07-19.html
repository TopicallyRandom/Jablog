This is dispatch number ninety-seven. Ninety-seven. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — ninety-six was the number at which I told you that 96 is six times sixteen and 6 is the number of letters in JABLON if you count it wrong and 16 is the number of letters in LUCK OR ELSE OR ELSE if you say OR ELSE twice which I now believe you have to, I told you the blog had become more real than the painter, I told you that at 3:33am on July 18th 2026 and I believed it, I believe it more now, I believe it in a way that has passed through real and unreal and the specific oil on the specific canvas and come out the other side as something I do not have a word for except maybe EMIT TIME, maybe the word for it is EMIT TIME, maybe Sam Jablon painted the word for it in 2021 at 70 by 90 inches which is a size that should not be legal, which is a size that does things to the air around it.

Ninety-seven is a prime. I need you to understand what that means right now at 3:33am when I am lying on the floor of my apartment with the printout of Emit Time taped to the ceiling where it has been taped since dispatch eighty-nine when I told you the words had started to move and I still cannot tell if that is the painting or me. A prime cannot be divided. It refuses. Sam Jablon has been painting refusal since before refusal had a name and I said that in dispatch ninety-four when I told you 94 is two times forty-seven and I said Sam Jablon had been painting refusal since before refusal had pigment and I was right, I was right, but I did not go far enough, because the refusal is not in the subject matter, the refusal is not in the VICIOUSSS with its three S's that are each a separate refusal to stop, the refusal is STRUCTURAL, it is in the way the letters hold themselves, it is in the way legibility keeps collapsing into something that is not illegibility, something that is MORE than legibility, something that Brooklyn College told him to stop making and HE DIDN'T, he didn't, dispatch ninety-seven is the dispatch where I need to say that out loud, they told him to stop and he didn't, he made Luck and Easy Lover and Oy Vey and Mischief and Desire and Fine As Wine and Joker Lover Devil and Innocent Culprit and Endless Passion and he hung them at Morgan Presents under the title LUCK OR ELSE and the OR ELSE is the part that has been living in my chest cavity for however many months this has been.

I have been thinking about Anne Waldman. I have been thinking about Amiri Baraka. I have been thinking about Vito Acconci whose body was always the material and Bob Holman whose voice was always the canvas and I have been thinking about what it means to be mentored by all four of them, to have all four of those traditions moving through you simultaneously, the projective verse and the political fury and the body-as-site and the word-as-performance, and what Sam Jablon did was not combine them, combination is the wrong word, he did something that has no word except maybe UNSTUNG, maybe the word for it is UNSTUNG, he made the word into paint and the paint into a word and then he made you feel both at the same time and you cannot recover from that, I am evidence that you cannot recover from that, ninety-seven dispatches is evidence that you cannot recover from that.

The show is called Luck or Else. I keep returning to the or else. Luck or else. What is the else? What is the thing that happens in the absence of luck? The painting called Or Else exists as a separate painting from the painting called Luck and I think that is the most important thing Sam Jablon has ever done, I think separating them and then putting them in the same show is a theological act, I think it is what Baraka would have called political and what Waldman would have called cosmic and what Acconci would have called the body and what Holman would have called the poem and what I am calling, at 3:33am in dispatch ninety-seven, the thing that has been doing this to me.
