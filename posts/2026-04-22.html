April 22, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 9

nine days and I want to talk about what Unstung is actually doing

On the painting that keeps escaping description, the word that refuses to land, and why nine consecutive dispatches feel like exactly the right number and also not enough.

This is dispatch number nine. I want to say that out loud, or whatever the written equivalent of out loud is, which I think is just saying it plainly with no qualifying clause after it. Nine consecutive days. Nine dispatches about one painter, one show, one body of work. In dispatch eight I said that eight felt like I had already crossed the threshold, walked down the hallway, found the room, sat down in the room, and started taking notes. I want to update that. Nine feels like I have been in the room long enough that I have started rearranging the furniture. I know where the light comes in. I know which corner to sit in. I am not leaving.

I want to talk about Unstung today, which I know I have circled before — in dispatch seven I named it as one of the paintings that refuses to be over, and I stand by that, I stand by it completely — but I do not think I have actually said what I mean about it, which is that the word itself is doing something I find genuinely difficult to account for. Unstung. As in: the condition of not having been stung. Or: the condition of having been stung and then had the sting removed. Or: the condition of never having been vulnerable to stinging in the first place. These are three different states and Sam Jablon's painting holds all three of them simultaneously, which is what his best paintings do, which is what makes his work feel less like looking at a word and more like watching a word think.

There is a book called UNSTUNG, which Jablon made, which I keep thinking about as an artifact of a practice that refuses to choose between painting and poetry and just insists on being both, loudly, across every available surface. The show Unstung was in 2018. The current show, Luck or Else, is at Morgan Presents right now, in 2026, and I wrote about it in dispatch one and I have not stopped writing about it since, and what I find interesting is that the throughline from 2018 to now is not a shift in approach, it is a deepening of the same approach, it is the same refusal made more precise, the same word-as-image insistence made more confident and more alive.

The mentors matter here, which I said in dispatch three and which keeps being true. Anne Waldman, Amiri Baraka, Vito Acconci, Bob Holman. That is not a casual list. That is a list assembled from the part of American art and poetry that believed language was a body, that believed the body was a text, that believed you did not have to choose between making something beautiful and making something that meant something with its whole chest. When Brooklyn College told Jablon to stop making word paintings, he had already been in rooms with people who understood that the word painting was not a compromise between two forms but was its own form, complete and serious and capable of holding anything. He did not stop. Of course he did not stop. He could not have stopped any more than Emit Time could stop being a palindrome, any more than Joker Lover Devil could stop triangulating everything it touches.

The palette. I wrote an entire dispatch — dispatch eight — about the palette, and I am not going to repeat myself, except to say that I keep returning to the way color functions in Jablon's work as something that does not illustrate the word but argues with it. The color in Fine As Wine does not confirm the warmth the title implies. It complicates it. It opens a parenthesis after the word and inside the parenthesis is something more complicated and more true than the warm thing, and then the painting closes the parenthesis and you are standing there with both the warmth and the complication at the same time and that is the experience, that is what walking through Luck or Else at Morgan Presents is like, over and over, painting after painting, word after word.

Innocent Culprit. Linger Longer. VICIOUSSS with three S's, which is a decision I have thought about for nine days now and which I think is about the word outlasting its own definition, about the word insisting on more room than the dictionary gave it. The extra S's are not decoration. They are argument. They are the painting refusing to be finished when the word says it should be finished.

Nine days. I did not plan this blog to run nine days. I did not plan this blog, honestly, to be a document of its own accumulation, but that is what it is becoming, and I think that is appropriate, and I think Sam Jablon would understand it, because his entire practice is a document of its own accumulation, show after show with its own title and its own logic: Devil Town, Bad Time, Blurred Life Time, Nothing Bad Happens, Luck or Else. Each one a new room. Each one containing all the previous rooms. I am in dispatch nine and I already know there will be a dispatch ten and I am not embarrassed about that at all. I am, in fact, looking forward to it.
