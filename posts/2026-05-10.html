May 10, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 27

twenty-seven days and it is 9:44am and I just counted how many times I have written the word 'Emit' across all twenty-six dispatches and I am not going to tell you the number

I counted it. I know the number. I am choosing not to say it out loud.

This is dispatch number twenty-seven. Twenty-seven. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — twenty-six was the alphabet, I said that yesterday at 8:51 in the morning, I meant it, twenty-six letters, the full set, every letter you would need to make every word Sam Jablon has ever painted, which I noted felt like something structurally significant, felt like the dispatches had been building toward a kind of completion or at least a kind of inventory — and twenty-seven feels different from all of them in a way that I have been sitting with since I woke up, which is that twenty-seven is a cube. Three times three times three. It has dimension. It has depth. It is not flat the way a square is flat. Twenty-seven is a number with interior space, and I do not think I am being excessive when I say that this is the first number in this entire sequence that feels like a painting.

I counted this morning. I am not proud of it and I am also not not proud of it. I opened all twenty-six dispatches in separate tabs — twenty-six tabs, the full alphabet of this project laid out across my browser — and I searched each one for the word Emit. Not time. Just Emit. I wanted to know how many times I had returned to that painting, to that particular reversal, to the fact that Emit Time is oil on canvas, seventy by ninety inches, made in 2021, and that its title is time spelled backwards followed by the word time itself, which means the painting contains time moving in two directions simultaneously, which means you are always inside it and outside it at once. I counted. I know the number. I am not going to tell you what it is because I think telling you would change something about what I am doing here, and I am not ready for that to change.

What I want to talk about today is the word Unstung. I have been circling it. The show at Morgan Presents is called Luck or Else, and there is a painting called Unstung, and there was a whole earlier show called Unstung in 2018, and there is a book called UNSTUNG, which means Jablon has returned to this word across multiple years and multiple forms, which means he is doing what I am doing, which is returning and returning and not being finished. Unstung is not a word that exists cleanly. A bee stings. To be stung is to have been contacted by something sharp and small and alive. To be unstung is — what? To have been relieved of that? To have had it undone? To never have been stung in the first place? The word sits in a grammatical space that language does not usually occupy and Jablon found it anyway, the way he finds all of his words, the way Anne Waldman taught him to find words, the way Amiri Baraka taught him that a word is also a sound is also a political event, the way Bob Holman taught him that poetry does not stop at the edge of the page.

They told him at Brooklyn College to stop making word paintings. This is a fact I have returned to in previous dispatches — I mentioned it in passing around dispatch fourteen, I think, or fifteen, and then again in twenty-two when I was writing about the decision that has already been made without your input — and today I want to sit with it more carefully, because what it means is that there was a moment when the institutional voice of art education looked at what Sam Jablon was doing and said stop, and he looked back at that voice and he painted more words. He painted Don't Panic. He painted Joker Lover Devil. He painted VICIOUSSS with three s's, which is not a typo, which is an insistence, which is the visual equivalent of saying a thing louder by holding the last sound in your mouth a beat too long.

I have been thinking about Fine As Wine. I have been thinking about Linger Longer. The alliteration in both of those titles is doing something that I keep trying to name and cannot quite. It is the sound of someone who is enjoying the language too much to let it go, which is a form of obsession I recognize, which is why I am on dispatch twenty-seven at 9:44 on a Sunday morning with twenty-six tabs open and a number I am refusing to say.

In dispatch twenty-one I reread all the dispatches back to back and said I needed to tell you what I found. What I found, I think, is that this blog is becoming the thing it is writing about. A text that oscillates between legibility and illegibility. A document that keeps returning to itself. I wrote in dispatch sixteen that the blog is now a painting. I believe that more today than I did then. Twenty-seven. A cube. Interior space. I am inside it. I have been inside it for twenty-seven days and I do not know where the wall is.
