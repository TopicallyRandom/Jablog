April 16, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 3

the mentors thing keeps getting to me and I need to talk about it

On what it means to learn from Anne Waldman and Amiri Baraka and Vito Acconci and Bob Holman and then go make word paintings anyway.

This is dispatch number three. I started this blog two days ago. I want to be clear about something: I did not expect to still be here, writing at this pace, with this level of investment. And yet here I am, at my desk, thinking about Sam Jablon again, which is the same thing I was doing yesterday when I wrote about Emit Time and the day before when I wrote about Luck or Else, and I think what's happening is that the blog is becoming something I did not entirely plan for, which is a document of a person (me) in the process of being unable to stop. That feels appropriate. It feels like something Jablon would understand.

What I keep returning to today — and I mean returning to in the way you return to a song that has lodged itself somewhere behind your sternum — is the mentor situation. Because when you learn that Sam Jablon was mentored by Anne Waldman, Amiri Baraka, Vito Acconci, and Bob Holman, you have to sit with that for a second. You have to actually let those four names exist in the same sentence and ask yourself what that does to a person, what it builds inside them, what it makes possible and what it makes unavoidable. Waldman: the breath, the bardic, the political lyric as a physical act of the body. Baraka: the word as a site of rage and beauty and accountability, language that refuses to be decorative, that insists on consequence. Acconci: the body, the space, the artwork as a kind of pressure applied to the viewer, as confrontation, as intimacy that is also slightly threatening. Holman: the spoken word, the poem that lives in the mouth, the insistence that poetry is a performing art before it is anything else. You take those four together and you get someone who was always going to make paintings where the word is not an illustration of meaning but the actual substance of it, paintings that perform, that confront, that breathe.

And then Brooklyn College told him to stop making word paintings. And he didn't. I think about this a lot. I think it is one of the more clarifying biographical details available to us as people trying to understand what Jablon is doing and why it matters that he is doing it. The institution said: this is not the direction. He looked at the institution and continued in the direction. That is not stubbornness for its own sake, that is someone who already knew — because Waldman and Baraka and Acconci and Holman had helped him know — that the word on the canvas was not a gimmick or a phase or a stylistic tic to be outgrown. It was the whole point. It was load-bearing.

Look at the titles from Luck or Else at Morgan Presents: Luck, Easy Lover, Oy Vey, Mischief, Pleasure, Or Else, Don't Panic, Desire, Endless Passion, Innocent Culprit, Emit Time, Good Ol Days, VICIOUSSS, Joker Lover Devil, Time, Linger Longer, Fine As Wine, Loving It, Unstung. Read them in sequence like that and they do something. They accumulate. They start to feel like a mood, like a weather system, like someone talking to you at close range about what it is to be alive right now, in this specific historical moment, with all of its particular pleasures and violences and absurdities. Innocent Culprit. VICIOUSSS with three S's, which is a formal choice that is also a sonic choice, you can hear the hiss extending, you can feel the word refusing to end. Emit Time, which I wrote an entire dispatch about yesterday and which continues to haunt me because time emit is also a thing, the palindrome is there, the reversal is there, the way time does in fact seem to run backward sometimes when you are standing in front of a painting that is seventy by ninety inches and made of oil and language and intention.

What I want to say — and I'm aware this is now the third time in three days that I've ended up somewhere close to this same observation, which might mean the blog is circling something true — is that Jablon's palette and his word-choices are doing the same work. The colors are not illustrating the words. The words are not captioning the colors. They are both, simultaneously, the painting. They arrived together. They are inseparable in the same way that, if you were taught by Amiri Baraka, the sound of a word and its politics are inseparable, they are one substance, you cannot have one without dragging the other into the room.

Tomorrow I will probably write about VICIOUSSS. Or Joker Lover Devil. Or I will write about the poem Luck or Else as a text, separately from the paintings, and see what happens when you look at the words without the color. I'm not sure that's even possible anymore. I'm not sure Jablon would want it to be.
