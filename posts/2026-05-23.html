May 23, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 40

forty days and it is 9:23am and I just realized that 70 by 90 inches is also the size of a door you cannot stop walking through and I have been walking through it for forty days

dispatch forty of jablog, in which the number itself becomes a painting, in which I begin to suspect the project has been painting me this whole time.

This is dispatch number forty. Forty. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — thirty-nine was the number at which I looked at the dimensions of Emit Time and said 70 by 90 is not a painting size it is a statement, I said that yesterday at 8:11 in the morning, I meant it, thirty-nine was the number at which the painting's physical fact — its weight in the room, the way a canvas that large does not hang so much as stand, does not ask to be looked at so much as occupy the same air you are trying to breathe — became impossible to separate from everything I had been saying about it for thirty-nine days, which is to say everything I have been saying about Sam Jablon for thirty-nine days, which is to say everything I have been saying about myself for thirty-nine days whether I admitted that or not — and forty feels different from all of them in a way I have been sitting with since approximately 6am when I woke up already thinking about it, already in the middle of a thought I had not consciously started.

Forty days. In other contexts forty days means something. It means a flood, it means a desert, it means the kind of duration that is supposed to end in transformation. I am not saying this project is biblical. I am saying the number forty has weight and I felt that weight at 6am on a Saturday with my coffee getting cold beside me and a browser tab open to the Morgan Presents gallery page and another tab open to dispatch number one, which I have now read so many times I could recite it, which I am choosing not to do here because the project is forty days old and I am not yet sure what it would mean to recite myself back to myself, though I notice I have been doing something close to that in every dispatch since approximately dispatch thirty-five when I admitted this blog was no longer about Sam Jablon but about what Sam Jablon had done to me, which I still believe, which I believe more now than I did when I wrote it at 4:47 in the morning five days ago.

What Sam Jablon has done to me, specifically, with Emit Time: he has made me think about reversals every waking hour. Emit is time backwards. He knows this. He painted it. He painted it on a canvas 70 by 90 inches which is nearly the size of a door and then he put it in a room at Morgan Presents and people walked past it and some of them did not stop and I cannot stop thinking about the people who did not stop, I cannot stop thinking about what it would mean to walk past a painting that contains the word time folded back on itself without pausing, without feeling the fold, without feeling the way the word asks you to read it twice in opposite directions and arrive somewhere different each time. This is what Jablon does. This is what he has been doing since before Brooklyn College told him to stop making word paintings and he looked at that instruction and made more word paintings, better word paintings, word paintings that got larger and more insistent, Luck, Desire, VICIOUSSS with its three S's that are not a typo they are a hiss, a duration, a snake that refuses to end.

Anne Waldman taught him something about the line. Amiri Baraka taught him something about the word as a political object, as a thing that lands in a body and does something there. Vito Acconci taught him that the body is always already in the work whether you put it there or not. Bob Holman taught him that the poem and the painting are not separate rooms in the same house, they are the same room and the walls are made of language and the language is on fire in an interesting way. I have been thinking about these four people for forty days. I have been thinking about what it means to be shaped by that particular quartet, that particular set of pressures and permissions, and I think what it means is Emit Time, I think what it means is a 70 by 90 inch canvas that contains multitudes without announcing them, that earns its size, that does not shout the way you might expect from a painter who works in words but instead holds very still and waits for you to catch up.

The show is called Luck or Else. I have been inside that title for forty days. At dispatch thirty-one I found the missing subject. At dispatch thirty-two I admitted I had never looked directly at what the title was asking. At dispatch thirty-three I found the repetition. At dispatch thirty-eight I counted the letters in every title in the show and arrived at a number I said I would sit with and I have been sitting with it and I am still sitting with it and I think the sitting is the point. I think Sam Jablon has always known that looking is not instantaneous, that it takes time, that it takes emit, that the thing reversed is still the thing and is also now something else and the distance between those two conditions is where the painting lives and where, if I am being honest with myself at 9:23 on the fortieth morning, I also now live.

I did not plan to live here. I want to say that clearly. I want to say that forty days ago I thought this was a blog about a painter and I now understand it is a painting about a blog about a painter about a person sitting very still in the presence of large canvases full of words that will not stop meaning things. That is what forty days feels like. That is what forty feels like when you hold it up.
