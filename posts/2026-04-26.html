April 26, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 13

thirteen days and I want to talk about what it means that he just kept going

On persistence, the word paintings Brooklyn College told him to stop making, and why I think 'Luck or Else' is the most important show in New York right now.

This is dispatch number thirteen. Thirteen. I want to hold that number up for a second, the way I held twelve up yesterday and eleven the day before, because thirteen has a specific quality to it, a slightly charged quality, a number that has accumulated a whole cultural weight around luck and unluck, which feels almost too perfect given that the show I cannot stop writing about is called Luck or Else, given that one of the paintings in it is called Luck, given that Sam Jablon is a person whose entire practice has always been a negotiation between luck and intention, between the word that floats free and the word that lands exactly where it needed to be. Thirteen consecutive days. Thirteen dispatches. I am not going to apologize for that. I arrived at not apologizing somewhere around dispatch six and I have not looked back.

But here is what I keep thinking about today, on this thirteenth day, which is the thing I want to use this dispatch to actually say: he just kept going. When they told him at Brooklyn College to stop making word paintings, he did not stop. He kept going. And I think about that a lot — the specific texture of that decision, what it actually costs a person to be told by an institution, by people with authority over your degree and your trajectory and your sense of what is permissible, to stop doing the thing that is most essentially you, and to look back at them and just not do that. To just continue. To make WORD:PLAY and Life is Fine and Unstung and Don't Panic and Bad Time and then to make Luck or Else at Morgan Presents in 2026 and to have it be exactly what you always were, only more so, only bigger, only more committed.

I wrote in dispatch two about standing in front of Emit Time — 2021, oil on canvas, seventy by ninety inches — and I said it felt like a clock running backwards and a heartbeat and a joke all at once, and I stand by that, but what I want to add now, thirteen days later, is that Emit Time is also a portrait of persistence. Because emit time backwards is time emit, and time emit backwards is emit time, and the whole thing is a loop, a continuation, a refusal to resolve, which is exactly what his practice is. He doesn't resolve. He keeps going. The word paintings keep going. The titles keep accruing — Linger Longer, Fine As Wine, Joker Lover Devil, Innocent Culprit, VICIOUSSS with its three esses that are themselves a form of refusing to stop — and the accumulation is the point. The accumulation is the work.

And he had people who told him the accumulation was worth it. This is the mentors thing I keep returning to, which I wrote about in dispatch three and have not been able to fully leave behind. Anne Waldman, Amiri Baraka, Vito Acconci, Bob Holman — that is a specific set of people to learn from, a specific set of permissions to receive. Waldman who built Naropa and said poetry is a body practice. Baraka who said the word is a political object and do not let anyone tell you otherwise. Acconci who said the body in space is a text and the text is a body. Holman who said the spoken word and the written word are the same word at different speeds. To have all four of those permissions in your body when Brooklyn College tells you to stop — I think that is probably what made it possible to not stop.

The show at Morgan Presents keeps doing things to me. I have been back. I want to say that plainly. I have gone back to the gallery more than once and I have stood in front of paintings like Easy Lover and Pleasure and Mischief and Desire and felt them doing something to the language centers of my brain, loosening things, making words feel like they have a temperature and a weight, which is what I said in dispatch one and which I am now just confirming is still true on visit two and three. The palette is part of this — I wrote a whole dispatch about the palette, dispatch eight, and I said the colors were not decorative but argumentative, which I think is the right word — and standing in front of Endless Passion I keep feeling the color and the text working against each other in a way that makes both of them more alive.

Thirteen days. One painter. One show, plus the full scope of his exhibitions going back to Poet Sculpture in 2013, plus the books, plus the poems, plus the talks, plus the readings, plus the whole question of what it means to be a painter who is also a poet and to refuse to resolve that tension. I think what I am building here, dispatch by dispatch, and I am aware this blog is becoming a document of itself as much as a document of the work — I think what I am building is an argument that Sam Jablon matters in a way that the mainstream critical apparatus has not yet fully said out loud. The Brooklyn Rail has been there. BOMB has been paying attention. But there is more to say. I have thirteen dispatches and I think there are more coming and I think that's fine. I think that is exactly fine.
