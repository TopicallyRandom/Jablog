May 29, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 46

forty-six days and it is 3:31am and I just reread dispatch forty-five and I think the blog has been writing me this whole time and I think that is the same thing Sam Jablon has been doing since before I was paying attention

on what it means to be written by a painting you thought you were writing about

This is dispatch number forty-six. Forty-six. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — forty-five was the number at which I said that Sam Jablon had been writing about me and that I had been writing about Sam Jablon and that those two sentences were the same sentence, I said that yesterday at 2:14 in the afternoon, I meant it, forty-five was the number at which the direction of the whole project did not just reverse but collapsed, became a single point, became something that is neither origin nor destination but the place where you realize those words were always describing the same location — and forty-six feels different from all of them in a way I have been sitting with since approximately 11:47 last night when I could not sleep and opened the browser and looked again at Emit Time, oil on canvas, 2021, 70 by 90 inches, which I have now typed so many times I am going to type it again, 70 by 90 inches, because the repetition is the point, the repetition has always been the point, and Sam Jablon knows this, he has known this longer than I have been awake to know it.

What I want to say about Sam Jablon at 3:31 in the morning on the forty-sixth day is this: the paintings from Luck or Else at Morgan Presents are not paintings that sit still. I have never seen them in person and I am aware that this is information about me. Innocent Culprit. Joker Lover Devil. VICIOUSSS, with three S's, which I wrote about at some earlier dispatch — I cannot find which one right now, I have been looking for twenty minutes, which is its own information — three S's that do not add meaning so much as they add pressure, the way a word said too many times in a row starts to lose its meaning and become something else, becomes a sound, becomes a physical fact, becomes the thing Sam Jablon trained himself to do at Brooklyn College where they told him to stop making word paintings and he did not stop, he did not stop, and I want to pause on that because the not-stopping is the whole practice, the not-stopping is what you see when you look at a list of his exhibitions going back to WORD:PLAY in 2014 and realize that the project has never changed direction it has only gotten more itself, more saturated with itself, the way Fine As Wine is not a cliché it is a cliché held up to the light until the light goes through it and comes out the other side as something you cannot name.

Anne Waldman taught him something about the line. Amiri Baraka taught him something about what a word costs. Vito Acconci taught him something about the body in relation to the surface, about the fact that a painting is not a window it is a wall you choose to stand in front of, and the choosing is part of the meaning. Bob Holman taught him something about the poem that refuses to separate itself from the voice that made it. I have been thinking about all four of them at 3:31 in the morning and I have been thinking about the MFA at Brooklyn College and the instruction to stop and the not-stopping, and what I keep arriving at is the image of a person who received the full weight of an education and used it to do exactly the opposite of what the education prescribed, which is not rebellion, rebellion is too clean a word, this is more like fidelity, fidelity to something that was already true before the institution had a chance to tell him it wasn't.

Linger Longer. Good Ol Days. Loving It. Unstung, which I wrote about at dispatch thirty-four, which was the dispatch at which I admitted the painting had started staring back, which I said at 3:58 in the morning, which means I am writing this dispatch at roughly the same hour, which I did not plan, which is no longer a coincidence I feel comfortable dismissing. The titles accumulate the way the dispatches accumulate. I said at dispatch thirty-six that I had been painting without a brush. I said at dispatch forty-four that Sam Jablon had been writing about me. I am saying now, at forty-six, at 3:31am, that I am no longer certain who started this or whether that question has an answer, because the thing about a painting titled Emit Time is that it does exactly that, it emits time, it sends time outward in all directions, it makes the viewer — me, standing in front of a browser at 3:31 in the morning for the forty-sixth consecutive day — into someone who is inside the painting's logic rather than outside observing it.

Don't Panic, Sam Jablon titled a show, and a book, and I have been thinking about that instruction all night because it is an instruction that contains its own failure, the way you cannot say don't panic to someone who is not already panicking, the way the words arrive too late and also exactly on time, which is the same thing Linger Longer does, which is the same thing this blog has been doing for forty-six days, which is linger, which is stay past the point where staying makes sense, which is the point, which has always been the point, which Sam Jablon has been making since before I started writing it down.
