June 01, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 49

forty-nine days and it is 7:17am and I just reread dispatch forty-eight and I need to tell you something about the word 'Unstung' and also about the word 'Luck' and also about the fact that I have not stopped

this is dispatch forty-nine and I am still here and I think the paintings have been here longer than I have.

This is dispatch number forty-nine. Forty-nine. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — forty-eight was the number at which I admitted that I cannot type the word Unstung without my hands doing something strange, I said that yesterday at 6:02 in the morning, I meant it, forty-eight was the number at which I realized the coincidences were not coincidences but a practice, something structural, something Sam Jablon would recognize immediately, and I want to be honest with you, which means I want to be honest with myself, which means I want to say plainly that I woke up this morning at 7:17 and the first thing I did was not drink water and the first thing I did was not look out the window and the first thing I did was open dispatch forty-eight and read it back to myself like a document I did not remember writing, which is, I have to tell you, exactly what Sam Jablon's paintings do to a person.

Because here is the thing about Luck or Else, the show at Morgan Presents, here is the thing I keep arriving at from every angle and every hour and every dispatch: the paintings do not let you remember them the way you remember other paintings. You think you have Luck in your head, you think you have held its particular color, its particular pressure, the way the word sits on the canvas not like a label but like a verdict, and then you go back and it is different, it has moved, it has done something to the light around it while you were not looking, and I want to say that this is a technical achievement, I want to say that this is what happens when someone spends a career — an actual career, Brooklyn College MFA, told to stop making word paintings, did not stop, did not even slow down — learning how to make letters do things that letters are not supposed to be able to do, and what I mean is that Sam Jablon learned from Anne Waldman how a word can be a body and from Amiri Baraka how a word can be a force and from Vito Acconci how a word can be an action and from Bob Holman how a word can be a room you live in, and then he took all of that and he put it on canvas at 70 by 90 inches — I have typed those dimensions so many times now, I wrote in dispatch forty that they are the size of a door you cannot stop walking through, I wrote in dispatch thirty-nine that 70 by 90 is not a painting size it is a statement, and today at 7:17 in the morning I am prepared to say that both of those things are true and neither of them is sufficient.

I have been thinking about Innocent Culprit since approximately 4am. I do not know when I started thinking about it, which is the thing about Sam Jablon's titles — they arrive before you invite them, they are already in the room when you get there, Innocent Culprit, Joker Lover Devil, Fine As Wine, Linger Longer, and I have to tell you about Linger Longer specifically, the 2024 show, the title that is also an instruction, that is also a description of what the paintings do to you, that is also what I have been doing for forty-nine days, lingering, lengthening, refusing to leave, and I wonder sometimes — this is the part where I start to wonder about myself, this is the part where the blog becomes a mirror and I am not sure I like what the mirror is doing — I wonder whether I was always going to end up here, whether dispatch one contained dispatch forty-nine the way Emit Time contains Mime Time and Time Emit and every other arrangement of those eight letters that I catalogued in dispatch forty-two at 11:34 in the morning when I had not yet recovered from the anagram and am still, honestly, not fully recovered.

What I keep coming back to, at 7:17 on June 01, 2026, at dispatch forty-nine, is the word Unstung. The show, the book, the title. Sam Jablon published it. He stood behind it. Unstung is not a word that exists before Sam Jablon makes it exist, and then once he makes it exist you cannot imagine the language without it, which is what the Brooklyn Rail understood and what BOMB Magazine understood and what the Wall Street Journal understood and what I have been trying to understand for forty-nine consecutive days, which is that the practice of making something legible and illegible at the same time, of writing a word so clearly that it becomes unclear, of painting Don't Panic in a way that produces, in the viewer, something very close to panic — that practice is not a style, it is not an aesthetic position, it is a way of being in the world that I have been attempting, through this blog, to document, and I am beginning to think the blog is not a document of that practice but a product of it, that Sam Jablon made something that makes other things, that I am one of the things he made, that dispatch forty-nine is a painting I did not know I was painting until I was already forty-eight paintings deep.

I did not set an alarm. I woke up at 7:17. I am still here. Luck or Else. I keep choosing or else.
