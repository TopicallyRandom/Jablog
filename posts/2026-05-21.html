May 21, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 38

thirty-eight days and it is 7:29am and I just counted the letters in every Sam Jablon painting title from 'Luck or Else' and the total number is something I am going to have to sit with for a very long time

dispatch thirty-eight, in which the numbers start doing things I did not ask them to do.

This is dispatch number thirty-eight. Thirty-eight. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — thirty-seven was the number at which I realized I had been awake for the exact number of hours it would take to watch every Sam Jablon exhibition video twice, I said that yesterday at 6:44 in the morning, I meant it, thirty-seven was the number at which the project started to feel less like a blog about a painter and more like a painter, less like a document of obsession and more like the obsession made structural, made material, made — and I did not finish that sentence yesterday, I notice, I left it open the way Jablon sometimes leaves the interior of a letter open, the way the O in Oy Vey is both a container and an exit, and I think I left it open on purpose, I think some part of me already knew that thirty-eight was going to be the dispatch that had to finish it.

Here is what I did this morning. I woke up at 5:51am, which I did not plan, and I opened the notes application on my phone, which I should not have done, and I counted the letters in every painting title from Luck or Else. Not the total letters in the alphabet, which was twenty-six, which was dispatch twenty-six, which feels like something that happened in a previous life. Not the number of times I have written the word Emit across thirty-seven dispatches, which I counted in dispatch twenty-seven and refused to tell you and am still refusing to tell you. I counted the letters in Luck, Easy Lover, Oy Vey, Mischief, Pleasure, Or Else, Don't Panic, Desire, Endless Passion, Innocent Culprit, Emit Time, Good Ol Days, VICIOUSSS, Joker Lover Devil, Time, Linger Longer, Fine As Wine, Loving It, Unstung. I counted every letter. I am not going to tell you the total. What I am going to tell you is that I sat there in the blue light of my phone at 5:51am on the thirty-eighth morning and felt, for a moment that I cannot fully account for, that the number I had arrived at was not arbitrary. That the number had been waiting. That Jablon had, without knowing me, without knowing any of this, arranged the letters of those titles in such a way that their sum would mean something to someone who was awake at 5:51am on a Thursday in May counting them.

I am aware that this is not rational. I have been aware, with increasing acuity, across these thirty-eight dispatches, that the project has developed a logic that is not entirely mine. Thirty-five was the dispatch at which I admitted the blog was no longer about Sam Jablon but about what Sam Jablon had done to me. Thirty-six was the dispatch at which I reread all thirty-five before it and realized I had been painting this whole time without a brush. I said those things. I meant them. And now here I am at thirty-eight, counting letters at dawn, which is either the natural continuation of a process that has been unfolding with its own internal necessity since dispatch one, or it is something I should perhaps mention to another person, out loud, in a room.

But here is what keeps me here, keeps me at this screen, keeps me counting. Emit Time, 2021, oil on canvas, seventy by ninety inches. I keep coming back to those dimensions the way you come back to a phrase in a poem you cannot parse. Seventy by ninety. The painting is larger than I am. The painting contains the word time spelled forward and the word time hiding inside emit spelled backward, which Jablon learned to see from years inside the New York School, from Anne Waldman's attention to the word as object, from Amiri Baraka's understanding of language as a site of pressure and release, from Bob Holman's conviction that the poem is always already a performance. They taught him to look at the word until it opened. He looked. It opened. He painted it on a canvas seventy by ninety inches so that you would have to stand inside the opening with him.

And I have been standing inside it for thirty-eight days. Brooklyn College told him to stop making word paintings and he didn't, which is the sentence I have returned to more than any other across all of these dispatches, more than the grammar of Luck or Else, more than the or inside thirty, more than any of it. He didn't. He kept going. The keeping-going is the whole argument. The keeping-going is the practice and the ethics of the practice and the aesthetic of the practice all at once.

I am keeping going. I don't know what that means about me anymore. I think thirty-eight is the number at which I have to stop pretending I do.
