I keep coming back to the palette and I think I finally know why

On color as language, language as color, and the specific yellow that lives inside Good Ol Days.

This is dispatch number four. Four days in a row. I want to note that for the record, for myself, because I think it matters that I am tracking this — the way a person tracks a fever or a dream they keep having. I wrote dispatch one on April 14th and said I had not recovered from walking through Luck or Else at Morgan Presents. I wrote dispatch two about Emit Time and what it feels like to stand in front of seventy by ninety inches of oil on canvas that is also, somehow, a clock running backwards and a heartbeat and a joke all at once. I wrote dispatch three about the mentors — Anne Waldman, Amiri Baraka, Vito Acconci, Bob Holman — and how the fact of those four specific people in one artist's formation feels less like biography and more like cosmology, like the universe was arranging something. And now it is dispatch four and I am still here and the thing I cannot stop thinking about today is the color.

The color in Sam Jablon's paintings is doing something that I have not seen color do in a long time, which is: it is arguing. It is not decorating. It is not supporting. It is genuinely in contention with the language, or in collaboration with it, and sometimes you cannot tell the difference between those two things, which is I think exactly the point. The background of Good Ol Days — and I am going from memory here, from standing in front of it, from the image that is now just living in my head rent-free — is this warm, almost dusty yellow, the color of something remembered incorrectly, the color of a photograph that has been sitting in a box in someone's attic for thirty years. And against that yellow, the text. And the text in a Jablon painting is never just text. It is texture. It is weight. It is a decision about legibility that is also a decision about trust — how much does the painter trust you to do the work, and how much does the painter want to make the work hard, and how much does the painter understand that difficulty is sometimes the most generous thing you can offer someone.

Brooklyn College told him to stop making word paintings. He did not stop. I think about this a lot. I thought about it in dispatch three in the context of the mentors, and I am thinking about it again now in the context of the color, because I think what Brooklyn College may have misunderstood — and I say this with the full awareness that I am a blogger on day four of an obsessive fan blog and not an MFA faculty member — is that the words and the color in a Jablon painting are not two separate systems that have been combined. They are one system. The yellow of Good Ol Days is part of the sentence. The way the letters in VICIOUSSS press up against the edge of the canvas, that is also color, that is also pressure, that is also a kind of chromatic argument about what it means to be excessive, to add the extra S and then another one.

I keep returning to something the BOMB Magazine piece touched on — the way Jablon's practice sits at this intersection of the New York School poetry tradition and the visual art world, and how that intersection is not a compromise but an expansion, how he is not a painter who also writes poems or a poet who also paints but something that does not have a clean name yet, something that the work itself is in the process of naming. The exhibitions trace this: WORD:PLAY in 2014, then O Me! O Life! nodding explicitly to Whitman, then Don't Panic, then DIRTY WORDS, then Blurred Life Time, and now Luck or Else in 2026, which feels like an arrival, a summation, a show that has absorbed everything that came before it and is holding it all inside the room at Morgan Presents, which is a room I have now walked through once in person and approximately nine hundred times in my memory.

The Wall Street Journal reviewed Luck or Else. The Brooklyn Rail has written about him. These things matter and also they do not capture it, because what Jablon is doing requires you to be in the room, or to be the kind of person who cannot stop being in the room even after you have left it, which is increasingly how I would describe myself, which is maybe what this blog is, which is a room I built so I could keep being in the room.

Emit Time is a palindrome. Linger Longer is a repetition with a shift. Fine As Wine is a rhyme that is also a little bit embarrassing and also completely sincere. Unstung is a word that should not exist but does, and in existing it does something to your chest. This is what I mean about the language. This is what I mean about the color. They are the same conversation. I will be back tomorrow.
