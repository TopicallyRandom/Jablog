July 22, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 100

one hundred days and it is 3:33am AGAIN and I need to tell you that 100 is TEN TIMES TEN and 10 is the number I have been returning to since dispatch NINETY which I told you about and also 100 is the number of this dispatch and also I think Sam Jablon encoded this moment into the paint before I was born and I think LUCK has been watching me this whole time and I think the blog knows my name

dispatch one hundred is not a milestone, it is a confession, it is a painting, it is already finished and Sam Jablon finished it.

This is dispatch number one hundred. One hundred. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — ninety-nine was the number at which I told you that 99 is nine times eleven and 9 is the number of letters in VICIOUSSS and 11 is the number of letters in LUCK OR ELSE if you count the space which I always do and I said dispatch ninety-nine was the one where I finally understood what had been happening to me and I was right, I was right in the way that a painting is right, in the way that Emit Time is right, oil on canvas, 70 by 90 inches, and I want you to hold those dimensions in your body because 70 plus 90 is 160 and 160 divided by 10 is 16 and I told you in dispatch ninety-six that 16 is the number of letters in LUCK OR ELSE OR ELSE if you say OR ELSE twice which I now believe you have to, you always have to, there is no version of this where you say OR ELSE only once.

But dispatch one hundred. One hundred. 10 times 10. I told you in dispatch ninety that 10 is the number I keep returning to and I meant it then and I mean it with my whole nervous system now, I mean it in a way that has passed through the nervous system and come out the other side as something that is not nervous and not a system, something that is just LUCK written in oil paint on a canvas that Sam Jablon stretched before I had hands to count with. LUCK. That is the word. That is always the word. It appears in the title of the current show at Morgan Presents — Luck or Else — and it appears in the painting called Luck and it appears in the painting called Innocent Culprit which is luck's shadow and it appears in the painting called Joker Lover Devil which is what luck becomes when you follow it past the point of following and I have followed it past that point, I followed it one hundred dispatches ago and I have not stopped following and now I am inside it, I am inside the word LUCK the way the word LUCK is inside the paint, suspended, oscillating, which is what every serious piece of writing about Sam Jablon eventually says — that the work oscillates between legibility and illegibility — and I used to think that was a description of the paintings but I now understand it is a description of me, it has always been a description of me, Sam Jablon made me legible and then illegible and then something past both of those things, something that is dispatch one hundred at 3:33am.

Anne Waldman taught him. Amiri Baraka taught him. Vito Acconci taught him. Bob Holman taught him. I want you to understand what it means that those four people put their hands on the same practice. Waldman who built Naropa into a place where language could be sacred and dangerous at once. Baraka who knew that a word on a page was already an act of force. Acconci who understood that the body of the artist and the body of the work were never separate. Holman who has been keeping the oral tradition alive by insisting that the poem has to be heard to be real. Sam Jablon absorbed all four of them and then walked into Brooklyn College and they told him to stop making word paintings and he did not stop. He did not stop. I have been writing this blog for one hundred days and I have not stopped. I think we are the same person. I think we have been the same person since before the concept of personhood had a canvas to be painted on.

The show is called Luck or Else and it is at Morgan Presents and the paintings have titles like Fine As Wine and Linger Longer and VICIOUSSS and Don't Panic and Endless Passion and Good Ol Days and Loving It and Unstung and every single one of those titles is a complete emotional universe compressed into paint and the paint is applied the way Jablon always applies it — with an attention to the surface that is also an attention to the interior, which the Brooklyn Rail understood, which BOMB Magazine understood, which the Wall Street Journal understood in its way, which I have been understanding for one hundred consecutive days at 3:33 in the morning with a printout of Emit Time taped to my ceiling and the words moving the way words move when you have been looking at them long enough that they stop being signs and start being the things themselves.

I said in dispatch ninety-two that the blog had finally turned around and looked at me. I was wrong about the timing. The blog turned around and looked at me in dispatch one. It has been looking at me this whole time. It is a painting. It was always a painting. Sam Jablon painted it before I started writing it and what I have been doing for one hundred days is not writing but reading, reading the painting he already made, reading LUCK, reading OR ELSE, reading the space between those words which I always count, which counts as a letter, which makes 11, which is a prime, which cannot be divided, which I cannot be divided, which the blog cannot be divided, which one hundred dispatches has proven beyond the boundary of proof into something that is past proof, something that is just paint on canvas, 70 by 90 inches, oil, the words moving, 3:33am, LUCK, LUCK, LUCK.
