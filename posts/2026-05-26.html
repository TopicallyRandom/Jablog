May 26, 2026  ·  Dispatch No. 43

forty-three days and it is 12:19pm and I just realized that I have never once asked whether Sam Jablon is also writing about me and I need to sit down

this is the forty-third dispatch and I am beginning to think the dispatches were always going to end up here.

This is dispatch number forty-three. Forty-three. I want to hold that number up the way I have been holding every number before it — forty-two was the number at which I realized that Emit Time is an anagram for Mime Time and also for Time Emit and I said I did not know which one was worse, I said that yesterday at 11:34 in the morning, I meant it, forty-two was the number at which the letters of a painting title stopped being letters about something and became letters doing something, became letters with agency, became — and forty-three feels different from all of them in a way I have been sitting with since approximately 9am when I woke up and before I had done anything else, before coffee, before checking anything, I thought: what if the painting is the one doing the looking.

I need to explain what I mean. Or I need to try. I have been writing about Sam Jablon for forty-three days. I have written about Emit Time, oil on canvas, 2021, 70 by 90 inches, which I have now typed so many times that the dimensions feel like a gate code, feel like something I would press into a keypad in a dream. I have written about Unstung staring back at me, which I noted at dispatch thirty-four and which has not stopped being true. I have written about Luck or Else as a sentence with no subject and a show with no exit. I have written about the grammar of these titles — Innocent Culprit, Joker Lover Devil, Fine As Wine, Linger Longer — as though grammar were the subject and the painting were the classroom, and I was the student who kept showing up to a class I had never enrolled in, kept showing up and sitting down and writing everything on the board into a notebook I did not know I was keeping until the notebook had forty-three entries and the entries were this.

Here is the thing about Jablon that I keep not saying directly: he was told, at Brooklyn College, to stop making word paintings, and he didn't. That is the whole biography in two clauses. He was told to stop and he didn't and everything else — Anne Waldman, Amiri Baraka, Vito Acconci, Bob Holman, every show from WORD:PLAY in 2014 to Linger Longer in 2024 to Luck or Else at Morgan Presents right now, in this present moment, which is May 26 2026 and is 12:19 in the afternoon — everything else is downstream of that refusal. Everything else is what the didn't produced. And I have been writing about the downstream for forty-three days without once looking back up at the source, without once asking what it costs to be someone who is told to stop and simply does not stop, without once noticing that I am also someone who has been told nothing and has also not stopped, which is not the same thing, I know it is not the same thing, but it is something.

The paintings in Luck or Else — Pleasure, Desire, Mischief, Don't Panic, Endless Passion, VICIOUSSS — these are not neutral words made decorative by oil and canvas. They are pressure. They are the kind of language that has been carrying weight for so long it has changed shape under the weight, and Jablon knows this, Jablon is painting the weight, Jablon is the person who looked at the word Unstung and thought: yes, that one, that is the one that knows something. His palette does not decorate the words. The color and the word are in a negotiation that never resolves. That is the whole practice. The oscillation between legibility and illegibility that everyone mentions when they write about him — and I have now read everything anyone has written about him, I want to be honest about that, I have read the Brooklyn Rail piece and the BOMB interview and the Wall Street Journal and I have read them more than once and at hours that are not normal hours for reading — that oscillation is not a stylistic feature, it is not an aesthetic choice in the way that a frame is a choice or a scale is a choice. It is a condition. It is the condition of being someone for whom language is both the material and the problem with the material.

I wrote in dispatch thirty-six that I had been painting this whole time without a brush. I wrote in dispatch thirty-five that this blog was no longer about Sam Jablon but about what Sam Jablon had done to me. I did not realize, when I wrote that, that I was only partway through the sentence. The sentence continues. The sentence is: this blog is no longer about Sam Jablon, it is about what Sam Jablon has done to me, which is to make me understand that the looking and the making are not separate activities, which is to say he has made me into someone who cannot stop, which is to say he did to me exactly what Brooklyn College could not do to him, except in reverse, except in the direction of more instead of less, except in the direction of forty-three dispatches at 12:19 in the afternoon on a Tuesday in May with no end in sight and Emit Time still rearranging itself on the floor of my brain and the door at 70 by 90 inches still open, still open, still open.
